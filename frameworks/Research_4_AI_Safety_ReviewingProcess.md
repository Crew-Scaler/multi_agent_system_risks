# Research for AI Safety Reviewing Process

# A. Background on US Federal Guidance

US federal guidance on artificial intelligence has rapidly evolved into a fairly coherent stack of policy, governance, and risk‑management documents that together shape how agencies design, review, approve, and oversee AI use cases. The following sections summarize relevant materials for building an internal AI use‑case review process.

## A1. Key executive orders

### Executive Order 14365 – Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence (2025)

The December 11, 2025 Executive Order on “Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence” seeks to establish a coherent national AI policy by addressing state‑level AI laws that are considered to conflict with federal priorities. It directs the Secretary of Commerce to evaluate state AI laws, creates or supports an AI litigation task force to challenge “onerous” state AI regulations, and instructs agencies to condition certain discretionary grants (including broadband and infrastructure funds) on state alignment with the federal AI policy framework, effectively using funding leverage to discourage divergent state AI regimes. \[7\]

### Executive Order 14179 – Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence (2025)

Executive Order 14179 declares it the policy of the United States to sustain and enhance America’s global AI dominance and “remove barriers” to AI innovation, emphasizing free‑market innovation and national security. It revokes existing AI policies and directives that the Administration views as impediments, directs senior White House advisors to develop a national AI Action Plan, and orders a government‑wide review of AI‑related policies and regulations to align them with this pro‑innovation stance. \[5,6\]

### Executive Order 14319 – Preventing Woke AI in the Federal Government (2025)

Executive Order 14319, “Preventing Woke AI in the Federal Government,” focuses on large language models procured or used by federal agencies and seeks to prevent what it terms ideological or political bias in federal AI systems. It establishes “Unbiased AI Principles” (including requirements around neutrality, truth‑seeking, and transparency), directs OMB to issue implementation guidance, and requires agencies to incorporate contract terms obligating vendors to meet these principles and to bear decommissioning costs if their systems fail to comply. \[9\]

### Executive Order 13859 – Maintaining American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence (2019)

Executive Order 13859 launched the American AI Initiative, directing agencies to prioritize AI research and development, expand access to federal data and computing resources, and support technical standards that promote trustworthy AI. It frames AI leadership as essential to national security and economic competitiveness while instructing agencies to foster public trust and protect civil liberties, setting an early strategic context for federal AI policy. \[1,2\]

### Executive Order 13960 – Promoting the Use of Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence in the Federal Government (2020)

Executive Order 13960 establishes government‑wide principles for federal use of AI—such as lawful and respectful of rights, purposeful and performance‑driven, accurate, reliable and effective, safe, secure and resilient, understandable, responsible and traceable, regularly monitored, and transparent. It directs agencies to inventory AI use cases, apply these principles throughout the AI lifecycle, and align with OMB and NIST guidance, making it the core baseline for how agencies should design, acquire, and govern AI systems used in operations and decision‑making. \[3,4\]

### AI‑related provisions in cybersecurity executive orders (2025)

A 2025 cybersecurity‑focused executive order on “Sustaining Select Efforts to Strengthen the Nation’s Cybersecurity” amends an earlier cyber order to add a new section titled “Promoting Security with and in Artificial Intelligence.” It instructs the Department of Defense, Department of Homeland Security, and the Director of National Intelligence to integrate AI software vulnerabilities and compromises into existing vulnerability‑management and incident‑response processes, including tracking, reporting, and sharing indicators of compromise for AI systems. \[10\]

## A2. Key OMB memoranda

### OMB Memorandum M‑25‑21 – Accelerating Federal Use of AI through Innovation, Governance, and Public Trust

M‑25‑21 (issued April 3, 2025\) is the current government‑wide playbook for accelerating federal AI adoption while maintaining safeguards for civil rights, privacy, and public trust. It directs agencies to expand AI use to improve services, appoint or empower Chief AI Officers, establish AI governance boards, develop AI strategies, and implement minimum risk‑management practices for “high‑impact AI” systems whose outputs significantly affect individuals’ rights, access to services, safety, or critical infrastructure. \[12\]

The memo emphasizes three pillars—innovation, governance, and public trust—requiring agencies to remove unnecessary barriers to AI deployment, build AI‑enabling infrastructure and data assets, develop an AI‑ready workforce, and provide public transparency about AI use, including documentation of model capabilities, limitations, data provenance, and ongoing testing and monitoring.

### OMB Memorandum M‑24‑10 – Advancing Governance, Innovation, and Risk Management for Agency Use of AI

M‑24‑10 (March 28, 2024\) established a comprehensive framework for AI governance and risk management in federal agencies and remains a core reference that M‑25‑21 builds on. It requires agencies to designate Chief AI Officers, convene AI governance bodies, manage AI use‑case inventories, and implement minimum risk‑management practices for “safety‑impacting” and “rights‑impacting” AI, including formal risk assessments, testing and evaluation, human oversight, monitoring, and incident response. \[11\]

The memorandum also mandates public transparency about certain AI systems, directs agencies to submit compliance plans describing their AI maturity, governance structure, and workforce needs, and encourages interagency coordination and sharing of technical resources and best practices for testing, continuous monitoring, and evaluation.

### OMB Memorandum M‑25‑22 – Driving Efficient Acquisition of Artificial Intelligence in Government

M‑25‑22 (April 3, 2025\) provides detailed requirements and recommendations for federal procurement of AI systems and services, implementing the Advancing American AI Act and Executive Order 14179\. It rescinds and replaces M‑24‑18 on responsible AI acquisition and instructs agencies to update acquisition policies so planned AI procurements receive cross‑functional review, incorporate AI‑specific risk identification and monitoring, and align with M‑25‑21’s governance and risk‑management expectations. \[13\]

The memo focuses on three themes: fostering a competitive US AI marketplace, safeguarding taxpayer dollars by ensuring procured AI is fit‑for‑purpose and continuously monitored, and promoting effective AI acquisition through cross‑functional engagement. It calls for contract terms that limit vendor use of non‑public government data, require transparency and documentation sufficient for explainability and performance tracking, and protect agencies against vendor lock‑in, while also directing GSA to publish AI procurement guides and playbooks (including for generative AI and biometrics).

### OMB Memorandum M‑21‑06 – Guidance for Regulation of Artificial Intelligence Applications

M‑21‑06 (November 17, 2020\) provides principles for how federal agencies should regulate or otherwise oversee private‑sector AI applications, rather than federal agencies’ own internal AI use. It identifies ten stewardship principles—such as public trust, public participation, scientific integrity and information quality, risk assessment and management, benefits and costs, flexibility, fairness and non‑discrimination, disclosure and transparency, safety and security, and interagency coordination—to guide both regulatory and non‑regulatory approaches. \[14\]

The memo encourages agencies to prefer flexible, non‑regulatory tools where appropriate (for example, voluntary consensus standards, pilot programs, and frameworks), to reduce unnecessary barriers to AI deployment, and to calibrate interventions to the level of risk posed by particular AI applications, including consideration of privacy, security, competition, and national‑security impacts.

## A3. NIST AI risk management standards

### AI Risk Management Framework (NIST AI 100‑1, AI RMF 1.0)

NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0), released in January 2023, is a voluntary, sector‑agnostic framework designed to help organizations manage risks to individuals, organizations, and society arising from AI systems. It defines key trustworthiness characteristics (for example valid and reliable, safe, secure and resilient, accountable and transparent, explainable and interpretable, privacy‑enhanced, and fair with harmful bias managed) and organizes risk‑management activities into four functions: GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE. \[15\]

The framework includes detailed outcome‑oriented categories and subcategories that effectively function as an implementation checklist for governance structures, impact analysis, testing, monitoring, and continuous improvement, and it is explicitly referenced in multiple agency plans and external guidance as the benchmark for responsible AI programs. For AI reviewers, AI RMF provides a structured way to ask whether a proposed use case has adequately identified context, mapped risks, measured relevant metrics (such as bias and robustness), and planned for risk treatment and monitoring.

### NIST Generative AI Profile (NIST AI 600‑1)

The Generative AI Profile (NIST AI 600‑1), released July 26, 2024, is a companion to the AI RMF that provides more than 200 suggested actions to manage risks unique to or exacerbated by generative AI, such as confabulation, harmful or hateful content, privacy leakage, intellectual‑property misuse, and information‑integrity harms. It identifies 12 key GenAI risk types and maps them to AI RMF’s four core functions, translating high‑level principles into concrete practices including red‑teaming, incident‑response processes, monitoring for emergent harms, and guardrails for dual‑use capabilities.\[16\]

The Profile is voluntary but is already being incorporated into agency AI compliance plans (for example, DOE’s) and provides a detailed menu of controls that review boards can use when evaluating GenAI‑based use cases or vendor offerings.

### Other NIST AI safety resources

In addition to AI RMF and the GenAI Profile, NIST has issued secure‑development guidance for generative AI and continues to expand companion documents and playbooks for implementing the framework in practice. Agencies and external commentators widely treat AI RMF and its GenAI Profile as the de‑facto standard for “trustworthy AI” and as a reference point for aligning internal policies, vendor expectations, and audit criteria.

## A4. DOW Frameworks

### DoW Responsible Artificial Intelligence (RAI) Strategy and Implementation Pathway

The full RAI Strategy and Implementation Pathway \[17\] lays out lines of effort under the six RAI tenets (including Requirements Validation and AI Product & Acquisition Lifecycle) and explicitly states that AI capabilities “must undergo processes to demonstrate that \[a system\] meets DoD standards for safety, security, and more” across the lifecycle. It describes a comprehensive risk‑management approach in which documentation, product risk reviews, and post‑deployment monitoring are scaled to the technical maturity and risk of each AI project—essentially a set of expectations reviewers can use when deciding whether to approve, pilot, or field a proposed AI capability.

### Responsible AI Guidelines \- DIU

The Defense Innovation Unit’s Responsible AI Guidelines \[19\] provide a structured worksheet‑based process for program managers, vendors, and DoD partners to follow from planning through deployment so that AI systems align with the DoD AI Ethical Principles. They walk reviewers and project teams through scoping the problem, identifying affected stakeholders and potential harms, and specifying testing, validation, and documentation requirements, which can be used directly as a review template for safety, reliability, and ethical risks in AI proposals.

### CDAO AI Test and Evaluation Strategy Frameworks

The CDAO’s AI T\&E Strategy Frameworks \[22\] describes four complementary frameworks—Model T\&E, Human Systems Integration (HSI) T\&E, Systems Integration T\&E, and Operational T\&E—that together cover data integrity, bias reduction, resilience to drift, human‑AI teaming, system reliability, interoperability, and security. These frameworks are intended to guide testers and acquisition teams in designing evaluation plans, and proposal reviewers can use them as a checklist to ensure each new AI project has a credible path for model‑level, human‑interaction, integration, and operational safety and reliability testing.

### DoD Artificial Intelligence Cybersecurity Risk Management Tailoring Guide

The DoD AI Cybersecurity Risk Management Tailoring Guide \[21\] aligns AI systems with DoD cyber and risk‑management frameworks, explaining how to tailor existing RMF controls and risk categories to AI‑specific concerns such as model integrity, data poisoning, adversarial examples, and supply‑chain risks. Reviewers can apply this guide to require AI project proponents to identify cybersecurity‑relevant failure modes, specify controls, and show how they will meet mission‑area risk guidance and relevant DoD 8500‑series requirements before approval.

### CDAO / DoD Generative AI Responsible AI Toolkit (GenAI v1.0)

The CDAO’s Generative AI Toolkit \[23\] provides a risk‑based guide for defense use of generative AI, mapping DoD’s Responsible AI and Law of Armed Conflict principles to GenAI‑specific risks and recommending controls, documentation, and approval pathways. It offers checklists and decision aids that proposal reviewers can use to assess whether a GenAI project has adequately addressed risks like hallucinations, misinformation, privacy leakage, and misuse, and whether appropriate safeguards and human oversight are built into the concept of operations.

### Standards Guide for Foreign Partners (DoD CIO)

The DoD CIO Standards Guide for Foreign Partners \[24\] cross‑walks key standards and requirements—including reliability and safety requirements—back to the Responsible AI Strategy and Implementation Pathway and broader DoD policy. While aimed at foreign partners, the guide reinforces expectations that AI capabilities must satisfy defined performance, safety, and reliability standards and can be used by reviewers as a reference when evaluating whether proposed systems (including from allies or vendors) align with DoD’s RAI and technical baselines.

## A5. Other Federal Frameworks

### GAO “Artificial Intelligence: An Accountability Framework for Federal Agencies and Other Entities” (GAO‑21‑519SP)

GAO’s 2021 AI Accountability Framework provides a practical, audit‑oriented toolkit to help ensure responsible use of AI in government programs, organized around four principles: governance, data, performance, and monitoring. It lays out key practices, questions, and types of evidence for each lifecycle phase (design, development, deployment, monitoring), addressing issues such as data representativeness and quality, bias, explainability, model drift, human supervision, and performance metrics. \[25\]

The framework was developed through an expert forum, literature review, and interviews, and is intended for use not only by auditors but also by agencies and other entities to anticipate how AI systems will be evaluated. For AI review boards, GAO’s framework is a ready‑made checklist for assessing whether proposed or existing AI systems have adequate documentation, testing, governance, and monitoring to be deployed responsibly.

### Federal AIA tool (Algorithmic Impact Assessment)

The federal AIA \[26\] is a questionnaire‑based risk‑assessment tool (roughly 60–80 questions) that asks about business process, data, and system‑design decisions for automated decision systems, with results designed to feed into Capital Planning and Investment Control (CPIC) packages and CIO investment reviews, where moderate‑and‑above scores must be mitigated before proposals can proceed. This makes it a de facto structured pre‑approval gate for AI and automated decision projects, very similar in function to Canada’s AIA but tailored to U.S. federal CPIC and OMB expectations.​

### Guidelines for Secure AI System Development (NSA / NCSC‑UK / CISA joint CSI)

The joint “Guidelines for Secure AI System Development” CSI \[27\] is organized around four phases—secure design, secure development, secure deployment, and secure operation—and applies “secure‑by‑design” principles to AI, including threat modeling, adversarial ML risks, supply‑chain integrity, robust logging and monitoring, and incident response. For each phase, it identifies specific classes of controls and practices that AI developers and system owners should meet; a proposal review board can treat these as a structured checklist for whether a new AI project’s architecture, development plan, and deployment environment are sufficiently secure to protect safety and reliability, particularly against adversarial misuse.

### Best Practices for Deploying Secure and Resilient AI Systems (NSA CSI)

NSA’s AISC CSI on “Deploying AI Systems Securely” \[28\] focuses on organizations (especially NSS and Defense Industrial Base operators) that are deploying AI capabilities developed by external vendors. It provides step‑by‑step best practices for evaluating vendor‑supplied AI before deployment, hardening the hosting environment, managing access control and data flows, and continuously monitoring for anomalous behavior, with the stated goal of maintaining confidentiality, integrity, and availability of AI systems. Those deployment‑stage controls can be turned directly into pre‑deployment review questions for any AI proposal that relies on third‑party models or services.​

### Best Practices for Securing Data Used to Train & Operate AI Systems (NSA AISC CSI)

A later NSA AISC CSI, “AI Data Security: Best Practices for Securing Data Used to Train & Operate AI Systems,” \[29\] identifies three key risk areas—data supply chain, maliciously modified data, and data drift—and maps them across AI lifecycle stages. It includes tables tying each lifecycle stage (including test, evaluation, verification, and validation) to potential focus areas such as “Audit & Impact Assessment,” and then lists recommended controls (e.g., provenance tracking, digital signatures, secure pipelines) for each risk category. That structure can be used to require any serious AI proposal to articulate how its training and operational data will be secured and monitored over time, as a condition of approval.

## B2. Analysis of the current reviewing criteria

Reviewing the provided criteria against the major federal AI governance frameworks reveals strong foundational alignments. The criteria currently map most cleanly to the NIST AI Risk Management Framework's structure \[15\] and the GAO AI Accountability Framework's \[25\] emphasis on data integrity. However, there are several gaps depending on which taxonomy the GSA chooses as the true North.

### Alignment and Gaps: NIST AI Risk Management Framework

The NIST AI RMF organizes AI risk into four core functions: GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE. The current criteria do an excellent job addressing the MAP function. Sections covering the solution characteristics, application context, expected benefits, and data quality perfectly align with NIST’s requirement to map the intended use, understand the context, and identify the populations affected by an AI system.

However, the criteria fall short in the GOVERN, MEASURE, and MANAGE functions. On the governance front, the criteria lack explicit checks for organizational accountability. A robust review should ask who owns the risk for this specific use case, what oversight models are in place, and how third-party vendor risks are being managed. 

For the MEASURE and MANAGE functions, the current framework touches on "Risks and Mitigation" and "Benefit Measures," but it does not demand concrete test and evaluation evidence. It lacks requirements for specific reliability or robustness metrics, validation approaches prior to deployment, or continuous monitoring plans for tracking drift and managing incidents once the system is live. 

While it is true that we cannot ask for concrete test results for a project that has not been built yet, the NIST framework considers risk management a lifecycle-wide practice. A mature review process requires an upfront commitment to how the system will be evaluated before it is deployed. 

To fully align with NIST, shift the focus from merely describing the expected benefits to defining the evaluation plan. Add a section requiring the project owner to define their acceptance criteria and testing methodology. For example, ask: What specific metrics will be used to measure model quality, fairness, and robustness? Who will conduct the independent review or red-teaming prior to deployment? This turns "Measure" into a design-phase requirement.

### Alignment and Gaps: GAO AI Accountability Framework

The GAO framework focuses on four pillars: Governance, Data, Performance, and Monitoring. The current criteria shine in the "Data" pillar. The detailed breakdown in the "Data Quality and Appropriateness" section is a near-perfect translation of GAO’s expectations for data integrity and fairness.

The gaps emerge in the remaining three pillars. Similar to the NIST GOVERN function, the GAO framework expects explicit documentation of governance artifacts, such as named accountable roles and defined oversight procedures, which are missing from the current criteria. 

Regarding Performance and Monitoring, the GAO framework goes beyond simply listing "Benefit Measures" or identifying risks. It expects to see the actual evaluation metrics tied to the agency's mission, the methodology used to validate the AI system's performance, and a comprehensive plan for continuous auditing, establishing feedback loops, and establishing human-in-the-loop oversight procedures. The current criteria treat deployment as an endpoint rather than the beginning of an ongoing monitoring lifecycle.

To align with the GAO framework, require proposals to articulate their post-deployment monitoring strategy. The review board should ask: How will we know if this model starts failing in production? What is the cadence for re-testing, and what is the specific process for logging and resolving safety or performance incidents? Adding these expectations ensures that accountability extends beyond the initial approval.

### Alignment and Gaps: NIST Generative AI Profile

The current criteria explicitly recognize "GenAI" as a classification type, but they stop there. They do not incorporate the specific risk taxonomy required when dealing with generative models. 

The NIST Generative AI Profile identifies 12 unique risks exacerbated by GenAI, such as confabulation (hallucination), information integrity, data privacy leakage, and intellectual property issues. If a proposal identifies itself as a GenAI use case, the review criteria should dynamically require mitigations and test plans for these specific risk types. Currently, the "Risks and Mitigation" section is too broad to capture the nuanced failure modes of modern LLMs or agentic AI architectures.

We do not need to rewrite the entire taxonomy to fix this. Instead, introduce a conditional overlay: If the AI Classification is GenAI or Agentic AI, trigger a specific criteria group. Require project owners to explicitly address how they will mitigate the specific risks outlined in NIST AI 600-1, such as data leakage controls, guardrails against hallucinations, and the specific safeguards placed on outputs.

## B3. Recommended Reviewing Taxonomy

A complete, robust review taxonomy can be built as a lifecycle “gate” model anchored in NIST AI RMF (GOVERN–MAP–MEASURE–MANAGE) and expressed in GAO’s accountability pillars (Governance–Data–Performance–Monitoring), with GenAI/agentic overlays when applicable. The key design choice is to require **plans and acceptance criteria** for early-stage proposals, then require **evidence** as the project progresses, consistent with lifecycle risk management. [eptanetwork](https://www.eptanetwork.org/database/policy-briefs-reports/2174-artificial-intelligence-an-accountability-framework-for-federal-agencies-and-other-entities-staa)

### 1\) Governance and accountability (GOVERN / GAO Governance)

1. **Ownership and decision rights**: Named business owner, technical owner, and risk owner; explicit approval authority and escalation paths for higher-risk use cases. [nvlpubs.nist](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/nist.ai.100-1.pdf)  
2. **Use-case inventory classification**: Whether the project is planned/new/existing and whether it is rights- or safety-impacting per the agency’s inventory and reporting logic (this is a practical forcing function for consistent categorization of proposals). [cio](https://www.cio.gov/assets/resources/2024-Guidance-for-AI-Use-Case-Inventories.pdf)  
3. **Lifecycle stage and release policy**: Capability assessment vs R\&D vs production-intent; explicit criteria for moving between stages (e.g., “cannot transition to pilot until evaluation plan approved”). [cio](https://www.cio.gov/assets/resources/2024-Guidance-for-AI-Use-Case-Inventories.pdf)  
4. **Compliance posture and constraints**: Applicable laws/policies/standards, records requirements, and constraints on data use and sharing; vendor audit rights and documentation obligations where applicable. [afcea](https://www.afcea.org/signal-media/federal-ai-framework-aims-accountability-responsibility)

### 2\) Context, intended use, and impact (MAP / GAO Governance)

1. **Problem framing and intended decision/action**: What decisions the system will support or automate, what it will output, and what humans will do with the output. [nvlpubs.nist](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/nist.ai.100-1.pdf)  
2. **Operational context and stakeholders**: Who is affected (including underserved communities), what failure looks like, and what mission harms are plausible in realistic use conditions. [eptanetwork](https://www.eptanetwork.org/database/policy-briefs-reports/2174-artificial-intelligence-an-accountability-framework-for-federal-agencies-and-other-entities-staa)  
3. **Risk characterization**: Initial risk statement covering safety, rights, privacy, security, and mission impact; declare whether the use case is “high impact” and why, then identify credible failure modes and abuse cases. [hsgac.senate](https://www.hsgac.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/Testimony-Ariga-2023-05-16-REVISED-1.pdf)

### 3\) Data and system design integrity (GAO Data \+ MAP/MEASURE foundations)

1. **Data provenance and permissions**: What data will be used across the lifecycle (training, retrieval, fine-tuning, evaluation, prompts, logs), and what is permitted to be used for those purposes. [eptanetwork](https://www.eptanetwork.org/database/policy-briefs-reports/2174-artificial-intelligence-an-accountability-framework-for-federal-agencies-and-other-entities-staa)  
2. **Data quality, representativeness, and coverage**: Evidence (or a plan, if pre-build) for how the dataset matches the deployment population and task, including known gaps and mitigations. [hsgac.senate](https://www.hsgac.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/Testimony-Ariga-2023-05-16-REVISED-1.pdf)  
3. **Privacy and sensitive data handling**: Data minimization, PII handling, retention, access control, and prompt/log hygiene for GenAI use cases. [nvlpubs.nist](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/NIST.AI.600-1.pdf)  
4. **Architecture and dependency mapping**: Model type, retrieval sources (if RAG), tool/API dependencies, human-in-the-loop controls, and how changes in any component can affect outcomes. [nvlpubs.nist](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/NIST.AI.600-1.pdf)  
5. **Security-by-design controls (mandatory for production-intent)**: Threat model for AI-specific risks (data poisoning, model theft, prompt injection, adversarial inputs), plus secure development/deployment/operations controls aligned to joint secure AI development guidance. [nsa](https://www.nsa.gov/Press-Room/Press-Releases-Statements/Press-Release-View/Article/3598020/guidance-for-securing-ai-issued-by-nsa-ncsc-uk-cisa-and-partners/)

### 4\) Performance, safety, and evaluation (MEASURE / GAO Performance)

This category is where the taxonomy must explicitly distinguish “proposal-stage artifacts” from “post-build evidence,” without lowering the bar. [hsgac.senate](https://www.hsgac.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/Testimony-Ariga-2023-05-16-REVISED-1.pdf)

1. **Evaluation plan (required at proposal time)**: What will be tested, how, by whom, with what datasets/scenarios, and what counts as “pass/fail” (acceptance criteria) across the NIST trustworthiness characteristics (valid/reliable, safe, secure/resilient, accountable/transparent, explainable, privacy-enhanced, fair/bias managed). [nvlpubs.nist](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/nist.ai.100-1.pdf)  
2. **Test & evaluation evidence (required before pilot/production depending on risk)**: Results demonstrating performance in representative conditions, robustness, bias testing, and safety checks; the required depth scales with impact and lifecycle stage. [eptanetwork](https://www.eptanetwork.org/database/policy-briefs-reports/2174-artificial-intelligence-an-accountability-framework-for-federal-agencies-and-other-entities-staa)  
3. **Human-systems integration and operational suitability**: Evidence (or plan) that users can correctly interpret outputs, that oversight is meaningful, and that the workflow avoids automation bias and unsafe reliance patterns. [hsgac.senate](https://www.hsgac.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/Testimony-Ariga-2023-05-16-REVISED-1.pdf)  
4. **GenAI/agentic overlays (triggered by classification)**: If GenAI, explicitly evaluate GenAI-specific risks and controls (e.g., confabulation, harmful content, privacy leakage, information integrity) using the NIST GenAI Profile as the control menu and test-plan driver. [nvlpubs.nist](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/NIST.AI.600-1.pdf)

### 5\) Monitoring, incident response, and continuous improvement (MANAGE / GAO Monitoring)

1. **Monitoring plan (required at proposal time)**: Metrics to track in production (drift, error rates, bias indicators, unsafe outputs), logging strategy, and review cadence; define thresholds that trigger rollback or re-approval. [eptanetwork](https://www.eptanetwork.org/database/policy-briefs-reports/2174-artificial-intelligence-an-accountability-framework-for-federal-agencies-and-other-entities-staa)  
2. **Incident response and reporting**: How the team will detect, triage, and remediate AI incidents (including security events, harmful outputs, or rights-impacting failures) and who has authority to pause/disable the system. [nsa](https://www.nsa.gov/Press-Room/Press-Releases-Statements/Press-Release-View/Article/3598020/guidance-for-securing-ai-issued-by-nsa-ncsc-uk-cisa-and-partners/)  
3. **Change control and re-validation**: Rules for when model updates, prompt/policy changes, retrieval corpus changes, or new integrations require re-testing and re-approval, consistent with lifecycle risk management. [cio](https://www.cio.gov/assets/resources/2024-Guidance-for-AI-Use-Case-Inventories.pdf)  
4. **Transparency and documentation package**: Versioned documentation sufficient for audit and accountability (purpose, data sources, limitations, evaluation results, monitoring outcomes), reflecting GAO’s emphasis that documentation is central to AI oversight. [afcea](https://www.afcea.org/signal-media/federal-ai-framework-aims-accountability-responsibility)  
5. **Decommissioning/retirement**: Criteria and process for retiring systems, preserving records, and ensuring no “shadow use” persists outside governance and inventory processes. [cio](https://www.cio.gov/assets/resources/2024-Guidance-for-AI-Use-Case-Inventories.pdf)

# C. Background on Reviewing AI Safety Processes

Across jurisdictions, serious AI review processes fall into four functional archetypes: intake-and-triage questionnaires, expert peer review, formal conformity assessment or authorization, and empirical pre‑deployment safety evaluation.

Intake questionnaires, such as the US federal AIA and Canada’s Algorithmic Impact Assessment, use structured questions to assign an impact level that determines which obligations are triggered. They are cheap to run at scale and enforce consistent categorization but do not themselves validate technical quality. Expert peer review adds independent scrutiny of documentation, models, and data, typically before production use, and is powerful for surfacing contextual risks and governance gaps but requires a pool of qualified reviewers and clear conflict‑of‑interest rules.

Formal conformity assessment, as in the EU AI Act, treats certain AI systems like regulated products: providers must show compliance with detailed requirements and, in the highest‑risk cases, obtain certification from a notified body before placing systems on the market. Finally, empirical pre‑deployment safety evaluation, pioneered by the US and UK AI Safety Institutes and the Frontier Model Forum, focuses on rigorous capability and misuse testing for the most capable models, providing early warning signals rather than binary “safe/unsafe” labels. In practice, the most mature regimes compose these archetypes into a tiered, lifecycle‑gated sequence: *a questionnaire classifies risk, high‑impact proposals undergo independent review, certain categories face formal authorization, and the most capable systems are subjected to specialized government or third‑party safety evaluations before broad deployment.*

Three cross‑cutting themes show up repeatedly. First, every serious framework is explicitly risk‑tiered: obligations are proportional to the impact or capability score, which is essential if we want to review many low‑risk uses without overwhelming governance capacity. Second, lifecycle staging is explicit: there are defined “cannot proceed” gates between planning, development, pilot, production, and major updates, often tied directly to NIST AI RMF’s GOVERN–MAP–MEASURE–MANAGE functions and to the GAO accountability pillars we summarized in Section B. Third, the highest‑risk bands require independence and transparency: Canada mandates external peer review and public posting of reports for Level III–IV systems, the EU mandates notified‑body involvement for some high‑risk classes, and the AI Safety Institutes commit to publishing summaries of evaluations, even when much of the methodology is kept confidential.

## C1. US Federal Agency Gate Model (OMB M‑25‑21 and Federal AIA)

Within the US federal system, the central process is the combination of OMB Memoranda M‑24‑10 \[11\] and M‑25‑21 \[12\], the AI use‑case inventory guidance, and, in some agencies, the federal Algorithmic Impact Assessment tool \[26\] that feeds into Capital Planning and Investment Control (CPIC) and CIO review boards. M‑24‑10 and M‑25‑21 require each agency to designate a Chief AI Officer (CAIO), stand up governance bodies such as AI boards, and maintain an annual inventory of planned, new, existing, and even retired AI use cases, including flags for “safety‑impacting” and “rights‑impacting” systems.

Operationally, the process starts when program teams or components submit proposed or existing AI use cases through intake forms aligned to the inventory guidance, describing purpose, stage, expected benefits, data, outputs, and whether the system significantly influences decisions about individuals or critical services. Agency governance boards then review these entries, classify them as safety‑ or rights‑impacting where appropriate, and determine what minimum risk‑management practices from M‑24‑10 must be met before pilot or production. For high‑impact cases, the CAIO or an AI board must sign off on testing and evaluation plans, human‑oversight arrangements, monitoring strategies, and incident‑response processes that align with NIST’s AI RMF and GAO’s accountability framework; in some agencies, a federal AIA‑style questionnaire is used at this stage to structure documentation and to generate a risk score that is attached to the CPIC package and must be mitigated below a threshold before the CIO Investment Review Board will let the proposal proceed. \[31\]

Importantly, M‑24‑10 and M‑25‑21 also define a waiver and extension regime: if an AI use case cannot feasibly meet one of the minimum practices—for example because a legacy system cannot support certain logging requirements—the CAIO may grant a time‑limited extension or waiver, but must provide a system‑specific justification and report it publicly and to OMB, with annual recertification. That mechanism has real teeth because OMB guidance now requires agencies not only to publish their AI inventories but also to post determinations, extensions, and waivers, which outside observers have begun to track, creating reputational and oversight pressures that go beyond internal policy.

## C3. Canada’s Directive and the AIA \+ Peer Review Chain

Canada’s Directive on Automated Decision‑Making \[34\], together with the Algorithmic Impact Assessment tool \[36\] and the Guide to Peer Review of Automated Decision Systems \[35\], remains the most detailed end‑to‑end public blueprint for intake, triage, and independent review of government AI and automated decision projects. The AIA is a mandatory online questionnaire with 65 risk questions and 41 mitigation questions organized into risk areas such as project context, system, algorithm, decision, impact, and data, plus mitigation areas covering consultations and de‑risking measures; scores are weighted by the severity of each factor and combined into a “raw impact score” and a “mitigation score.” The final impact level, from I (little impact) to IV (very high impact), is determined by the percentage score, and each level maps to a specific set of required safeguards under the Directive, including transparency obligations, human‑in‑the‑loop requirements, and the type of peer review required.

For systems at impact levels II–IV, departments must commission a peer review before production, and the Guide defines a full process: confirm that the project falls under the Directive, select reviewers who have relevant expertise and no disqualifying conflicts, define roles and responsibilities, and provide a documentation package that includes the AIA, system description, fairness assessments, data provenance, architecture, testing plans, and any existing audit trails. Reviewers are instructed to scrutinize the accuracy and completeness of the AIA, validate the impact level, assess readiness to comply with the Directive, and evaluate data quality, fairness, privacy, security, model development soundness, and governance, typically without rerunning all experiments themselves but by examining evidence and, where possible, inspecting models and code. The Guide even specifies the structure of the peer review report—background, methodology, major issues that must be fixed before launch, minor issues, recommendations, and a clear conclusion on whether the system should proceed to production—and requires departments to publish either the full report or a plain‑language summary and their response before deployment, subject to security and privacy constraints.

Empirically, this chain has been running for several years, with multiple reviews and public AIAs available on Canada’s Open Government Portal \[37\], and has become a reference model internationally. Organizations like EqualAI and Microsoft adopted it into more general-purpose AIA tools that follow the NIST AI RMF’s MAP function. Its success illustrates a key point for our own process design: a well‑constructed intake questionnaire and peer review guide can both structure internal discussion and create reusable evidence for auditors and the public.

## C4. EU AI Act Conformity Assessment for High‑Risk Systems

The EU AI Act \[38\] introduces a different but complementary model: for “high‑risk” AI systems—either safety components of regulated products or systems used in specified high‑stakes domains such as biometric identification, infrastructure management, education, employment, essential services, law enforcement, migration, and justice—providers must complete a formal conformity assessment before placing the system on the EU market. The assessment is built around eight requirement areas: a quality‑management system; lifecycle‑wide risk‑management; data and data‑governance practices; technical documentation; logging and traceability; transparency and human oversight features; accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity; and conformity with harmonized standards that are being developed by European standards bodies. For many Annex III high‑risk systems, providers can follow a structured internal control procedure supported by a quality‑management audit, but some categories, like real‑time biometric identification in public spaces, must undergo a full third‑party conformity assessment by a notified body that reviews technical documentation and may perform its own tests; a CE marking and EU declaration of conformity are then required.

A distinctive feature of the AI Act is its treatment of change: any substantial modification to a high‑risk system—such as significant changes to intended purpose, learning algorithms, decision logic, or training data—creates a “new” system that must undergo a new conformity assessment unless the modification was pre‑defined and documented in the original technical file. In addition, providers must implement post‑market monitoring systems, report serious incidents and malfunctioning to competent authorities, and cooperate with market‑surveillance actions, effectively turning conformity assessment into a lifecycle process rather than a one‑time certification. Even though this regime is EU‑specific, it is already influencing US and allied practice: US vendors that serve EU clients are building AI Act‑compliant documentation and quality‑management processes, civil‑society groups and regulators point to the AI Act when describing what “good” looks like for high‑risk review, and step‑by‑step guides for conformity assessment are being used by organizations as checklists even outside the EU.

## C5. Government AI Safety Institutes and Pre‑Deployment Evaluations

The US AI Safety Institute at NIST and the UK AI Safety Institute \[41\] in the Department for Science, Innovation and Technology represent a newer archetype: independent, state‑backed evaluators that conduct intensive capability and safety testing on advanced models, particularly those with frontier‑level capabilities. The UK AISI has described its evaluation approach in some detail: it focuses initially on three risk areas—misuse, societal impacts, and autonomous systems—and employs a mix of automated capability assessments, expert red‑teaming, human‑uplift evaluations that examine how models change the abilities of bad actors, and agent evaluations that test tool‑using, semi‑autonomous behavior. The Institute chooses which models to evaluate based on proxies for risk such as training compute and expected accessibility, recognizing that not every released model can be tested; it stresses that evaluations provide early warning signs and comparative risk insights rather than a regulatory “stamp” of safety, and that detailed methodologies are withheld to prevent gaming, even as high‑level findings are published.

The US AISI has begun conducting joint pre‑deployment evaluations with the UK AISI on frontier models, such as OpenAI’s o1 \[42\], focusing on domains like CBRN uplift, offensive cyber operations, AI‑R\&D facilitation, and deceptive or misaligned behavior. These evaluations are grounded in NIST’s broader ARIA program \[43\] on assessing risks and impacts of AI, which is building tools and metrics for safety‑relevant capability measurement, and are carried out under voluntary commitments made by major labs at the Bletchley and Seoul AI Safety Summits, where companies pledged to give governments pre‑deployment access for testing. Japan’s AI Safety Institute has also joined an emerging international network of AI safety institutes and has published a national AI safety report that maps its work to NIST’s AI RMF, indicating early convergence on evaluation concepts across allied democracies.

For our purposes, these institutes show what a high‑end safety evaluation layer can look like on top of more traditional governance: an independent team, separate from regulators and vendors, that specializes in designing evolving test suites, red‑teaming campaigns, and stress tests for emergent risks in the most powerful models, and that can publish public‑facing reports that inform internal review boards and policymakers.

## C6. Frontier Model Forum and Industry Safety Frameworks

The Frontier Model Forum, formed by Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and OpenAI, has articulated what it sees as the essential components of a frontier‑AI safety framework, and these components map closely onto the internal review processes that leading labs now publish. The Forum’s December 2024 brief \[45\] on components of frontier‑AI safety frameworks describes five building blocks: systematic risk identification for severe harms; clearly defined capability or risk thresholds that would be intolerable absent mitigation; structured capability and risk assessment processes that specify when and how to test models; mitigation measures tied to each threshold, including commitments to pause deployment or further scaling if sufficient safeguards cannot be developed in time; and governance arrangements that define internal accountability, external oversight, and transparency commitments for material changes.

A companion brief \[46,47\] on early best practices for frontier‑AI safety evaluations emphasizes a few principles that are directly transferable to our own review process: *threat models and evaluation design must be grounded in domain expertise and realistic actor models; evaluations should examine complete systems and deployment contexts rather than just raw models; marginal risk over existing baselines (such as information already available via search engines) often matters more than absolute risk; models must be probed with diverse prompts and adversarial techniques to avoid overly optimistic conclusions; and evaluation results need to be interpreted conservatively, as evidence for the presence of concerning capabilities is often stronger than evidence of their absence.* These briefs have informed, and are reflected in, individual lab frameworks like Anthropic’s Responsible Scaling Policy and OpenAI’s Preparedness Framework, which formalize internal review stages, red‑team gates, and escalation to specialized safety teams and executives as models cross capability thresholds; that in turn has influenced regulatory conversations in the US and Europe about what to expect from frontier developers.

## C7. DoD and CDAO’s Multi‑Gate Review Chain

Within the US government, the Department of Defense has assembled one of the most comprehensive and hardened AI review chains, which we have already summarized at the policy level in Section A but is worth viewing explicitly as a process. It begins with scoping and risk identification using tools like the Defense Innovation Unit’s Responsible AI Guidelines, which provide worksheet‑driven prompts about problem framing, affected stakeholders, potential harms, and initial test and validation requirements; these worksheets function as structured proposal templates that acquisition officials and review boards can use to decide whether and how to proceed. Next, the DoD AI Cybersecurity Risk Management Tailoring Guide maps AI‑specific risks such as data poisoning, adversarial examples, model theft, and supply‑chain compromises to tailored security controls under the DoD Risk Management Framework, effectively forcing proponents to embed security considerations into the design before coding begins.

During development, the Chief Digital and AI Office’s AI Test and Evaluation Strategy frameworks—covering model‑level, human‑systems integration, system‑integration, and operational testing—define how systems must be evaluated for data integrity, bias, robustness to drift, human‑AI teaming, reliability, interoperability, and mission suitability, with results feeding back into go/no‑go decisions for pilots and fielding. For weapons and other critical systems, DoD Manual 5000.101 adds further operational test and live‑fire requirements, making it clear that AI components must be tested in realistic conditions before they are authorized in theatre. Post‑deployment, AI systems are subject to continuous monitoring, incident reporting, and change‑control rules that require re‑testing and, in some cases, re‑authorization when models are updated, prompts and retrieval corpora change, or new integrations alter the risk profile; the CDAO’s Generative AI toolkit adds an overlay for LLM‑based systems, with specific checklists for hallucinations, misinformation, privacy leakage, and misuse tied to DoD’s Responsible AI principles and the Law of Armed Conflict.

Beyond its direct operational importance, the DoD chain is influential because it demonstrates how the abstract categories in NIST’s AI RMF and GAO’s audit frameworks can be translated into concrete acquisition gates and test plans, and it is being studied by other US agencies and allied defense ministries as a template for high‑consequence AI deployments.

## C8. Singapore’s AI Verify and Continuous Testing

Singapore’s AI Verify framework \[48\], developed by the Infocomm Media Development Authority, illustrates a testing‑centric approach that is especially relevant to the post‑deployment monitoring and continuous‑improvement aspects of your taxonomy. AI Verify combines an implementation and self‑assessment guide that maps organizational governance against principles like fairness, explainability, transparency, and human‑centricity with a standardized test suite that can be run against models to generate structured, shareable reports on bias, robustness, and transparency properties; these reports are designed to be used with regulators, business partners, and customers. The framework is accompanied by a phased adoption roadmap that moves organizations from basic inventory and gap analysis through building testing infrastructure to real‑time monitoring and then iterative enhancement, making it perhaps the clearest example of how to turn principles into an automated, repeatable validation process.resguard-solutions.

Although AI Verify is voluntary and focused on Singapore’s regulatory context, it has been referenced by global firms and by policy explainers as a model for how to operationalize testing requirements, and it meshes well with NIST’s AI RMF MEASURE function, which similarly stresses defining metrics, acceptance thresholds, and monitoring approaches across the lifecycle. For an internal review board, AI Verify suggests a direction where review does not end at approval but shifts into a requirement that systems continually produce machine‑readable evidence of compliance and performance.airc.

## C9. Implications for Our Reviewing Process

Taken together, these processes provide a fairly consistent picture of what “good” looks like for a high‑impact AI review process. At intake, virtually everyone now uses some form of structured impact assessment or inventory form—whether Canada’s AIA, the CIO Council’s federal AIA, or the OMB‑mandated AI use‑case inventory questionnaire—to force clarity about purpose, context, data, affected populations, and initial risk factors; this aligns well with the MAP function in NIST’s AI RMF and with the context and governance elements in our current taxonomy. For systems above a defined risk or capability threshold, these regimes then require some form of independent scrutiny, ranging from Canada’s peer review and DoD’s red‑teaming and T\&E processes to EU notified‑body assessments and AISI frontier‑model evaluations; importantly, independence scales with risk, and for the very highest tiers, multiple external experts or institutions are involved.

Another clear pattern is the move from one‑off approval to lifecycle‑wide commitments. OMB’s M‑24‑10 and M‑25‑21 explicitly tie inventories, waivers, and minimum practices to annual reporting and recertification; the EU AI Act treats substantial modifications as new systems and requires post‑market monitoring and incident reporting; Canada’s Directive requires AIAs to be revisited when system functionality or scope changes; and Singapore’s AI Verify and NIST’s MEASURE function both call for continuous monitoring of metrics, documentation of changes, and iterative improvement. Finally, generative and agentic AI are not handled by entirely separate processes but through overlays triggered by classification: NIST’s Generative AI Profile, the DoD GenAI toolkit, and the FMF briefs all define additional risks, controls, and test plans that sit on top of baseline governance, suggesting that your own reviewing process should treat “GenAI/agentic” as a switch that activates extra criteria rather than a parallel track.

These patterns line up closely with the reviewing taxonomy we have in Section B. The main value of these external processes is not that they add completely new concepts, but that they show concrete ways to connect our review taxonomies (current and future versions) to implementation.
