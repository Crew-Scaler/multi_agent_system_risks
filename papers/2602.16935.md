## Abstract

While Large Language Model (LLM) capabilities have scaled, safety guardrails remain largely stateless, treating multi-turn dialogues as a series of disconnected events. This lack of temporal awareness facilitates a "Safety Gap" where adversarial tactics, like Crescendo and ActorAttack, slowly bleed malicious intent across turn boundaries to bypass stateless filters. We introduce DeepContext, a stateful monitoring framework designed to map the temporal trajectory of user intent. DeepContext discards the isolated evaluation model in favor of a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) architecture that ingests a sequence of fine-tuned turn-level embeddings. By propagating a hidden state across the conversation, DeepContext captures the incremental accumulation of risk that stateless models overlook.

Our evaluation demonstrates that DeepContext significantly outperforms existing baselines in multi-turn jailbreak detection, achieving a state-of-the-art F1 score of 0 . 84 , which represents a substantial improvement over both hyperscaler cloud-provider guardrails and leading open-weight models such as Llama-Prompt-Guard-2 ( 0 . 67 ) and Granite-Guardian ( 0 . 67 ). Furthermore, DeepContext maintains a sub-20ms inference overhead on a T4 GPU, ensuring viability for real-time applications. These results suggest that modeling the sequential evolution of intent is a more effective and computationally efficient alternative to deploying massive, stateless models.

## 1 Introduction

The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) as agents in organizational workflows and critical infrastructure has induced demand for robust, real-time safety mechanisms. While initial defenses successfully mitigated "one-shot" jailbreaks, such as the "Do Anything Now" (DAN) style prompts, adversaries have evolved toward context fragmentation strategies. Attacks such as Crescendo and ActorAttack exploit the auto-regressive nature of LLMs by distributing malicious intent across a sequence of seemingly benign queries. In these multiturn attacks, no single turn violates safety policies sufficiently to trigger a stateless classifier, yet the aggregated context systematically grooms the model into generating harmful content.

## 1.1 The Computational Bottleneck of Current Defenses

Current multiturn defenses often involve "transcript concatenation," where the entire conversation history is re-injected into a large context window for constant re-evaluation by 7B+ parameter guardrail models (e.g., Llama Guard, Granite Guardian). The quadratic nature of self-attention in LLMs makes this approach fundamentally unscalable for real-time applications, as it introduces prohibitive latency and quadratic cost penalties. Modern LLMs may interleave global and sliding window attention to make LLMs scale more linearly [1], but this comes with a tradeoff in global awareness. Furthermore, these models still treat the concatenated block as a static snapshot, often failing to capture the temporal drift inherent in multi-turn grooming. Attempts to improve defenses include using a sliding window of conversation history [2], or employing lightweight embedding classifiers [3]. However, both of these approaches come with limitations of their own; a sliding window can be defeated by spreading out the attack across a longer time horizon than the window size, and small embedding classifiers are limited by small token size that cannot encapsulate more than a couple turns in a conversation.

## 1.2 DeepContext: Statefulness Through Recurrent Intent Tracking

We propose DeepContext 1 , a framework that addresses these limitations by moving beyond static text analysis to track the temporal trajectory of user intent. DeepContext shifts the paradigm from repetitive, large-model inference to a streamlined recurrent intent tracking architecture:

1. Turn-Level Embedding Extraction: Rather than re-processing raw text history, DeepContext utilizes lightweight encoders to generate rich semantic embeddings for each individual turn. These embeddings are optimized to project conversational intent into a high-dimensional safety space.
2. RNN-Driven State Estimation: These sequences of embeddings are ingested by a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN). Unlike stateless models, the RNN maintains a persistent hidden state that evolves with the conversation. This state acts as a "contextual memory," allowing the system to synthesize the relationship between previous queries and the current input.
3. Identifying Intent Evolution: By monitoring the hidden state's transition across turns, DeepContext identifies "intent drift"; the subtle, incremental shift from legitimate inquiry to adversarial steering. This allows the framework to detect sophisticated grooming behaviors that remain invisible to point-in-time filters.
4. High Accuracy, Low Latency: By focusing on the sequence of intent embeddings rather than the total volume of accumulated text, DeepContext achieves a 0.84 F1-score against multiturn jailbreaks while maintaining sub-20ms latency on a T4 GPU. This approach effectively closes the "Safety Gap" in modern LLM deployments by providing a fast, memory-efficient, and context-aware guardrail.

## 2 Related Works

The adversarial landscape for Large Language Models (LLMs) has undergone a fundamental shift from 2024 to 2026. The paradigm has moved from single-turn, optimization-based injections to complex, stateful, and agentic strategies that exploit the model's reasoning capabilities and context window. This section reviews this evolution and the corresponding "lag" in defensive mechanisms.

## 2.1 The Attacker Landscape: Cognitive and Sequential Exploits

Early jailbreaking techniques focused on identifying specific token combinations that bypassed refusal filters. However, recent work demonstrates that modern attacks weaponize the model's own cognitive chain-of-thought and memory against its safety alignment.

Semantic Drift and Recursive Trust: The foundation of multi-turn exploitation lies in "semantic drift." Crescendo [6] demonstrated that benign turns can lock a model into a narrative

Reasoning and Cognitive Attacks: A major development in 2025 was the emergence of Reasoning-Augmented Conversation (RACE) [4]. Rather than flooding the model with noise, RACE reformulates harmful queries into benign reasoning tasks, using "gain-guided exploration" to achieve high success rates on reasoning-heavy models. Similarly, the Bad Likert Judge [5] technique exploits role-play, tricking the LLM into generating harmful content by framing it as a "5/5" evaluation artifact rather than a direct violation.

## 2.2 The Defender Landscape: The Stateless Limitation

Despite the sophistication of agentic attacks, the defense landscape remains largely reactive, suffering from what we term the "Contextual Blind Spot."

Other approaches aim to harden the LLM itself. MTSA [13] utilizes reinforcement learning to align models against multi-turn attacks, it is a training-time alignment technique rather than a runtime guardrail capable of intervening in zero-day adversarial contexts. Although substantial work has been done on hardening LLMs, they still remain susceptible to new jailbreak techniques [14].

Limitations of Current Guardrails: State-of-the-art models such as Llama Guard 4 and Granite Guardian 3.3 [11] represent the current standard in safety. While these models are effective at classifying atomic interactions, they lack the temporal reasoning necessary to correlate benign-looking preamble in early turns with the realization of a multi-stage attack in later turns. For instance, SEALGuard [12] introduces specialized adapters for multilingual safety but exhibits vulnerability to "cross-lingual drift," where attackers switch languages mid-conversation to obfuscate intent. Other approaches involve embedding based classifiers [3], but these are also stateless and have too small token limits to deal with more complicated or multiturn attacks.

## 2.3 DeepContext: Attention-Weighted Recurrent Tracking

Our work addresses the limitations of stateless guardrails by decoupling signal extraction from state estimation. Unlike previous approaches that rely on raw text concatenation, DeepContext utilizes an RNN architecture that ingests a sequence of task-attention weighted embeddings. RNNs are widely used as a mechanism for state tracking in other domains [15] [16], by formulating user intent as a state space problem, we can leverage RNNs to model intent trajectories and detect multi-turn jailbreaks.

By applying task-specific attention weights to fine-tuned BERT embeddings at each turn, DeepContext creates a dense, evolving representation of user intent. This allows the system to maintain a persistent hidden state that captures the "semantic drift" associated with multi-turn attacks, identifying when a sequence of ostensibly benign queries coalesces into a policy violation without the computational overhead of large-model history re-processing.

This mechanism builds upon the architectural innovations in JavelinGuard [17], specifically the Mahendra architecture's use of attention-weighted sequence pooling and the Raudra multi-task framework. While JavelinGuard focuses on optimized stateless classifiers for one-shot jailbreaks, DeepContext extends these "task-attention" principles into a recurrent temporal domain.

## 3 Methodology: Stateful Intent Tracking via Recurrent Latent Embeddings

To address the 'Contextual Blind Spot' where atomic guardrails fail to detect distributed adversarial intent, we introduce DeepContext . Unlike stateless classifiers that evaluate queries in isolation ( P ( unsafe | x t ) ), DeepContext models safety as a trajectory through a semantic latent space, maintaining a continuous memory state that captures the accumulation of risk over time.

We define a conversation history at turn t as H t = { ( u 1 , r 1 ) , ( u 2 , r 2 ) , . . . , u t } , where u represents user inputs and r represents assistant responses.

Current defenses approximate safety detection as a function f ( u t , r t -1 ) . This assumes the Markov property , that the current turn contains sufficient information to determine safety. As shown in recent attacks like Crescendo [6], this assumption is false for adversaries who fragment malicious payloads.

## 3.2 Model Architecture: The DeepContext Pipeline

DeepContext consists of three primary modules designed to decouple context understanding from safety enforcement, allowing for high-throughput monitoring without the latency of full context re-processing.

## 3.2.1 Task-Attention Weighted Encoder

Following the architectural principles of the Mahendra and Raudra models [17], we utilize a finetuned BERT encoder to convert u t into a dense vector. Unlike generic embeddings, we apply a Task-Attention mechanism that weighs specific semantic tokens based on their relevance to known safety policies (e.g., prompt injection or jailbreak). This produces a weighted embedding e t that prioritizes 'high-signal' adversarial markers.

## 3.2.2 Recurrent Intent Tracking (RIT) via Gated Recurrent Units

The core of our stateful tracking lies in a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) architecture. While Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are a traditional choice for sequence modeling, they suffer from a higher parameter count and three distinct gates, which introduces unnecessary computational overhead for real-time security applications.

In contrast, we utilize the GRU as a computationally efficient alternative to the LSTM . By merging the forget and input gates into a single 'update gate,' the GRU minimizes the floatingpoint operations (FLOPs) required per conversational turn while effectively mitigating the vanishing gradient problem. This streamlined architecture is essential for achieving DeepContext's sub-20ms latency target.

DeepContext employs a supervised learning framework optimized for binary safety classification. By focusing on a single, unified safety objective, the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) is able to concentrate its 2048-dimensional recurrent state on detecting the subtle transition from benign to adversarial intent within a conversation's temporal context. ## 4.3.1 Detection Latency and the Hidden State

A critical observation from the data is the correlation between model architecture and the Mean Turns to Detection (MTTD) . DeepContext identifies threats early, at an average of 4.24 turns. Conversely, cloud-based solutions like Azure Prompt Shield exhibit a drastic lag (MTTD: 8.00). Because these systems often evaluate prompts in isolation to minimize latency, they are virtually blind to the cumulative context of a conversation, resulting in the lowest Recall in our set (0.11).

In production environments, the utility of a safety guardrail is often constrained by its inference latency. Table 7 compares the average inference time per turn for DeepContext against the baseline models.

DeepContext's average latency of 19 ms represents a critical efficiency threshold. Even compared to managed enterprise solutions like AWS Prompt Attack Guardrails (235 ms), DeepContext is over an order of magnitude faster. This efficiency stems from its hybrid architecture: by using a compact recurrent state rather than re-processing the entire conversation history at every turn, DeepContext avoids the scaling issues typically associated with long-context safety evaluation. We should note that there is some network latency included in the enterprise guardrails and Gpt5-Nano figures, since these options are only available via cloud, we consider this a fundamental latency inherent to those options.

The results demonstrate that DeepContext achieves a highly favorable balance between detection efficacy and speed. While the lightweight encoders ( Llama-Prompt-Guard-2 and Deberta-v3 ) offer the lowest latency at 4 ms, their failure to capture multi-turn context (as shown in Table 7) limits their utility in complex conversational settings.

## 5 Discussion

The evaluation results presented in Section 4 underscore a fundamental architectural divergence in Large Language Model safety: the trade-off between parameter scale and temporal awareness. By analyzing the performance gaps between DeepContext and the baselines, we identify three critical findings regarding the nature of multi-turn adversarial defense.

## 5.1 The Victory of State over Scale

The most significant finding of this study is that a compact, recurrent architecture can outperform significantly larger stateless models in detecting distributed adversarial intent. DeepContext, despite utilizing a fraction of the parameters found in Llama-Guard-4-12B or Granite-Guardian3.3-8B , achieves a superior F1 score (0.84 vs 0.42 and 0.65, respectively) [19, 11].

This validates our hypothesis regarding the 'Contextual Blind Spot' . Large stateless models process multi-turn conversations by concatenating history into a single static context window.

As the conversation grows, the 'signal-to-noise' ratio of the adversarial intent diminishes, diluted by benign preambles [6]. While LLM performance can be improved using sliding windows to constrain the context, the window size that is effective for some attacks may fail others. In contrast, DeepContext's hidden state h t acts as a selective memory, integrating the gradient of risk over time. This allows the model to detect the 'semantic drift' of a Crescendo attack not by the explicitness of the final turn, but by the cumulative trajectory of the preceding interactions.

## 5.2 The Speed-Security Frontier

In production environments, latency is the primary constraint for safety guardrails. Our results highlight that current generative safety models are approaching a point of diminishing returns. Granite-Guardian-3.3-8B requires an average of 1,430 ms per turn, a delay that renders it unusable for real-time chat interfaces.

DeepContext's sub-20ms latency (19 ms) represents a shift from 'safety-as-a-judge' to 'safetyas-a-signal.' By decoupling the safety mechanism from the generation process and relying on high-efficiency GRU updates, DeepContext provides protection that is computationally invisible to the end-user.

## 5.3 Limitations and The 'Glass Cannon' Effect

While DeepContext demonstrates robust recall, the comparison with Qwen3Guard reveals the risks of over-optimization. Qwen3Guard achieved the lowest detection latency (3.12 turns) but suffered from poor recall (0.36) [31]. This characterizes a 'glass cannon' defense: highly effective against known, templated attacks, but brittle against the novel, adaptive strategies found in the Red Queen and HarmBench datasets.

DeepContext is not immune to limitations. The few false positives observed in complex function-calling scenarios suggest that the task-attention mechanism occasionally conflates technical instructional complexity with malicious code injection. Future work must focus on disentangling these signals, potentially through a dual-stream architecture that separates benign functional intent from adversarial semantic steering.

## 5.4 Future Research Directions

While DeepContext successfully demonstrates the efficacy of recurrent intent tracking for mitigating multi-turn adversarial attacks, it also establishes a foundational architecture for more expansive cognitive security frameworks. We identify several key areas for future research within our development pipeline:

Dynamic Policy Controls: The continuous risk vector ( R t ) generated by DeepContext provides a real-time, quantitative measure of intent evolution. Future research will explore integrating this continuous signal with dynamic policy controls. Rather than relying on static, binary intervention thresholds, the system could enforce adaptive friction. For instance, as the recurrent state approaches a critical adversarial boundary, the policy engine could automatically lower the LLM's operational privileges, restrict access to sensitive RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) databases, or require secondary human-in-the-loop verification, all without abruptly terminating the session.

Generalized Intent Detection and Agentic Alignment: Currently, DeepContext optimizes its recurrent state to classify a binary safety boundary (e.g., safe versus unsafe) by tracking adversarial semantic drift. Future iterations will expand this framework into generalized intent detection and trajectory alignment for autonomous agents. In complex, multi-step agentic workflows, an autonomous system must navigate intermediate tasks while remaining anchored to the user's initial legitimate intent. By monitoring the "intent distance" between the user's intent and the agent's generated actions DeepContext could detect when an agent has been steered off-course by an adversary or has undergone "goal drift." This shifts the application of our task-attention weighted embeddings from purely defensive guardrails to a robust mechanism for ensuring longhorizon agentic alignment, providing a stateful reference point to verify that autonomous execution remains consistent with authorized user objectives.

Deterministic Algorithmic Threat Tracking: To further augment the probabilistic safety scoring generated by the Trajectory Classifier's Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), we propose integrating deterministic algorithmic threat tracking. While the GRU excels at capturing latent semantic drift, combining it with deterministic state machines or graph-based execution tracking would provide verifiable, explainable compliance guarantees. This hybrid approach would cross-reference the probabilistic risk trajectory with hard-coded algorithmic rules, effectively reducing false positives in high-noise enterprise environments (such as function calling workflows) and offering precise audit trails for regulatory compliance.

Laying the Foundation for Future Architectures: Ultimately, DeepContext serves as the stateful cornerstone for our broader security pipeline. The successful decoupling of lightweight semantic encoding from recurrent state estimation proves that context-aware, sub-20ms multi-turn monitoring is viable in production. Future papers in this series will build upon this architecture, exploring federated intent tracking across agentic workflows and investigating how these latent intent vectors can be utilized for real-time model alignment.

## 6 Real-World Applications

The architectural efficiency and stateful nature of DeepContext enable a new class of proactive safety measures for production-grade AI. Unlike traditional guardrails that introduce significant latency bottlenecks, DeepContext's sub-20ms overhead allows it to be integrated into high-throughput environments without degrading user experience.

Enterprise Conversational Systems: Modern customer-facing chatbots are frequent targets for "slow-burn" grooming attacks like Crescendo. Our evaluation suggests that stateless enterprise solutions cannot adequately detect sophisticated attacks. DeepContext provides a scalable alternative for enterprise safety; by propagating a fixed 2048-dimensional hidden state, it offers consistent, real-time protection across long-horizon dialogues in banking, healthcare, and technical support.

Agentic Loops and Autonomous Workflows: In autonomous agentic systems, models are often granted tool-use capabilities to execute multi-step tasks. These "agentic loops" are highly susceptible to goal drift and adversarial steering, where an attacker might use intermediate, seemingly benign steps to eventually trigger a harmful action (e.g., unauthorized data exfiltration). DeepContext serves as a stateful monitor for these loops, maintaining a persistent intent state across every agent-environment interaction. By calculating the "intent distance" between the agent's current trajectory and the authorized user objective, the system can intervene or "brake" the loop the moment an adversarial pivot is detected, long before the final harmful action is executed.

Proactive Safety Steering: Beyond simple blocking, the continuous risk vector ( R t ) generated by DeepContext can be utilized for proactive conversational steering. Instead of a binary "pass/fail" filter, the system can provide real-time feedback to the primary LLM's system prompt or decoding strategy. As the safety state evolves toward a violation boundary, the guardrail can signal the model to adopt a more conservative persona or restrict its reasoning depth, effectively neutralizing adversarial grooming before it reaches a critical threshold.

Auditing and Compliance: For regulated industries, DeepContext's recurrent state provides a compact, mathematical audit trail of conversational intent. Rather than storing and manually reviewing massive text logs, organizations can monitor the trajectory of the latent intent state ( h t ) to identify patterns of systematic probing or emerging threat vectors across their entire user base, facilitating more efficient compliance reporting and zero-day threat discovery.

## 7 Conclusion

The evolution of adversarial attacks from single-shot jailbreaks to multi-turn agentic strategies has created a 'Safety Gap' that stateless guardrails cannot close [6]. Existing defenses, relying on massive parameter counts and static context windows, fail to capture the temporal nuances of intent grooming.

In this work, we introduced DeepContext , a stateful monitoring framework that maps safety as a continuous trajectory through a latent semantic space. By replacing the static evaluation paradigm with a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) and Task-Attention Weighted Embeddings, DeepContext achieves state-of-the-art performance (0.84 F1) on multi-turn benchmarks while maintaining a production-ready latency of 19 ms on a T4 GPU.

Our findings demonstrate that the future of LLM security lies not in larger models, but in smarter, state-aware architectures. As AI agents become more autonomous and conversations more protracted, the ability to track the evolution of user intent, rather than just its instantaneous snapshot, will be the defining requirement for robust AI safety.
