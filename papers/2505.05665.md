## Characterizing the Robustness of Black-Box LLM Planners Under Perturbed Observations with Adaptive Stress Testing

Neeloy Chakraborty, John Pohovey, Melkior Ornik, and Katherine Driggs-Campbell

University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign {neeloyc2,jpohov2,mornik,krdc}@illinois.edu

## Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated success in decision-making tasks including planning, control, and prediction, but their tendency to hallucinate unsafe and undesired outputs poses risks. This unwanted behavior is further exacerbated in environments where sensors are noisy or unreliable. Characterizing the behavior of LLM planners to varied observations is necessary to proactively avoid failures in safety-critical scenarios. We specifically investigate the response of LLMs along two different perturbation dimensions. Like prior works, one dimension generates semantically similar prompts with varied phrasing by randomizing order of details, modifying access to few-shot examples, etc . Unique to our work, the second dimension simulates access to varied sensors and noise to mimic raw sensor or detection algorithm failures. An initial case study in which perturbations are manually applied show that both dimensions lead LLMs to hallucinate in a multi-agent driving environment. However, manually covering the entire perturbation space for several scenarios is infeasible. As such, we propose a novel method for efficiently searching the space of prompt perturbations using adaptive stress testing (AST) with Monte-Carlo tree search (MCTS). Our AST formulation enables discovery of scenarios, sensor configurations, and prompt phrasing that cause language models to act with high uncertainty or even crash. By generating MCTS prompt perturbation trees across diverse scenarios, we show through extensive experiments that offline analyses can be used to proactively understand potential failures that may arise at runtime. Code is available at https://sites.google.com/illinois.edu/astllm/ .

## 1 Introduction

With the success of large language models (LLMs) in areas like conversational chat, questionanswering, and embodied AI, researchers have begun deploying these models in reasoning-heavy planning tasks (Bommasani et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2024). More recent studies have evaluated LLMs in autonomous driving, pick-andplace robotics, and unstructured outdoor navigation (Zeng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). Unlike web-based LLM agents (Debenedetti et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025), real-world planners rely upon noisy sensors or perception algorithms. Researchers may finetune the model or give few-shot examples to minimize the domain gap and adapt to new scenarios.

Nevertheless, like any large neural network, LLMs have few guarantees on how they may act in unseen conditions. In particular, while showcasing impressive feats, LLMs are known to hallucinate and confidently produce undesired generations that can conflict with hard constraints, lead to suboptimal plans, or are completely irrelevant to the task at hand (Chakraborty et al., 2025). Detecting hallucinations and estimating the uncertainty of LLM planners is key, especially when evaluating such models in safety-critical settings. Existing hallucination detection methods can be classified based on the availability of information from the model-under-test, ranging from white-box (model weights, hidden states, output token probabilities, and generations are accessible) to black-box models (only generations are accessible).

Asubset of algorithms seek to understand model limitations by generating adversarial prompts that elicit undesired behaviors under edge cases. Adversarial prompt generators for white-box models typically require the gradient of the token outputs of the language model under test to find influential prompts (Wallace et al., 2019; Shin et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2024). Methods for black-box models like RoboPAIR (Robey et al., 2025) generate jailbreaking prompts with potentially hallucinated details using an LLM. Existing adversarial prompt injection techniques applied to web-based LLMs study the response of models to a change in the ground-truth, underlying state, like adding a phrase to an email to lead the model to click on a malicious link (Wang et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025), which is analogous to optimizing the ground-truth trajectories of adversarial cars on a road, causing a crash. In contrast, the noisy and unpredictable nature of real-world environments necessitates algorithms that characterize the response of LLMs to varied scenarios, prompt phrasing, and observations. 1

In response to this need, we develop a rule-based generator that produces realistic prompts grounded in the observation space of the environment by applying perturbations from two different dimensions. One dimension applies common perturbations like randomizing order of details to characterize model sensitivity to varied prompts with the same semantic meaning, while the other dimension explores the effect of the available observation details and sensor noise to understand robustness to sensor failures. To efficiently search the perturbation space, we formulate adversarial prompting as an adaptive stress testing (AST) task (Lee et al., 2015; Corso et al., 2019; Du and DriggsCampbell, 2021; Hardy et al., 2025) and we intelligently search the prompt perturbation space in a black-box manner with Monte-Carlo tree search (MCTS) ( ´ Swiechowski et al., 2023). Our framework enables researchers to (1) automatically and systematically characterize the response of an LLM to perturbations in varied situations, and use the characterization to proactively (2) identify scenarios where the LLM is unreliable, and (3) generate prompts that impact LLM uncertainty or cause it to act unsafely.

Our contributions are as follows: (1) We manually apply a series of prompt perturbations in a driving environment and show the tendency of LLMs to hallucinate; (2) We present a novel framework grounded in AST for systematic characterization of LLMs and (3) analyze model tendencies in three safety-critical deployments; (4) We show that the offline robustness characterization can be used to proactively (a) generate perturbed prompts that impact LLM uncertainty and decision modes and (b) identify potential cases where an LLM is likely to act with high uncertainty in real-time.

## 2.1 Foundation Model Planners

While classical controllers and learning-based methods work particularly well for seen distributions, they may have unintended consequences when deployed to an out-of-distribution environment (Wen et al., 2024b). More recently, researchers are deploying large foundation models as planners, acting under the assumption that their enormous training set distribution allows these models to generalize to unfamiliar circumstances (Zeng et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024). LLMs in particular have achieved promising results when deployed as planners in complex, multistep decision-making tasks like autonomous driving (Hwang et al., 2025; Mao et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024) and robotics (Driess et al., 2023; Ichter et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024). We rigorously evaluate the robustness of such models to perturbations to gauge whether they should truly be trusted to act autonomously in safety-critical deployments like driving, moon landing, and robot navigation.

## 2.2 Hallucination Detection and Mitigation

Chakraborty et al. (2025) define hallucinations as generations that conflict with hard constraints ( e . g ., an infeasible robot action), deviate from desired behavior ( e . g ., colliding with another agent), or are completely irrelevant. We are particularly interested in identifying a subset of hallucinations under noisy observations and rephrased prompts where the planner (1) is uncertain of its decision and generates inconsistent predictions or (2) consistently predicts actions that lead to critical failures. Hallucination intervention methods for white-box LLMs may train a classifier with hidden states from the language model, identify a space of adversarial prompts with gradient-based prompt perturbations, or directly finetune the model (Lin et al., 2022; Azaria and Mitchell, 2023; Yao et al., 2023). For grey-box LLMs, researchers have devised methods to correlate hallucinations with output token probabilities and calibrate confidence scores with conformal prediction (Ren et al., 2023; Hazra et al., 2024). Many methods for black-box LLMs sample multiple generations from the model to check for similarity or consistency, ground generations to an external database, or identify infeasible plans (Jha et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023; Park et al., 2024; Yoon et al., 2025). Our proposed two-dimensional per- turbation space basis goes beyond standard prompt augmentations and simulates realistic sensor failures that arise at runtime on real-world robots. Another group of works use black-box adversarial prompting techniques to induce hallucinations (Ramakrishna et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024; Robey et al., 2025). These approaches often feed the language model incorrect information or invalid questions, which will surely lead the model to produce incorrect outputs. In contrast, our framework is designed to generate prompts grounded in the original observation space of the model, leading to more informative insights on different perturbations. Finally, we note that hallucination intervention for language model planners is an open area of research, as most works are evaluated in question-answering domains at this time (Chakraborty et al., 2025).

## 2.3 Adaptive Stress Testing

To characterize the sensitivity of LLMs to varying scenarios and prompt perturbations, we use adaptive stress testing. AST is a framework for finding worst-case conditions that cause a model-under-test to fail. Notably, by formulating LLM characterization as an AST task, we enable researchers to define a custom undesirability function based on behaviors that should be found during characterization. In this work, we experiment with finding scenarios, prompt phrasing, and observation configurations that cause the LLM to produce unstable responses or crash. Researchers have applied the framework in different domains like autonomous driving and airplane collision avoidance (Lee et al., 2015; Corso et al., 2019; Du and Driggs-Campbell, 2021). Hardy et al. (2025) propose learning an adversarial prompting language model with AST to elicit toxic responses from a model-under-test, but their method requires access to token probabilities, and it is not deployed in a decision-making context. While we constrain our work to characterizing uni-modal language models, we are encouraged by the results from Julian et al. (2020) who use AST to perturb image modalities, leading us to believe that the framework could be extended to multi-modal LLMs or vision-languageaction models in the future.

## 3 Manual Perturbation Case Study

We first perform a case study to analyze how LLMs behave when deployed as decision-making agents in a multi-agent driving environment, and evaluate the impact of manually perturbing prompts.

## 3.1 Language Models as Drivers

Suppose a language model m is acting as a driver in a highway environment e , like the one used by Wen et al. (2024a). We model this problem as a partially observable Markov decision process with the tuple ⟨S , A , O , P , R , Z , γ, S 0 ⟩ , where S is the state space of the environment, A is a set of actions m can take, O ⊆ S is the space of observations available to the driver, P is the simulator's transition model, R is the reward function, Z is the observation function, γ ∈ (0 , 1] is the discount factor, and S 0 ⊆ S is the set of initial states. At every timestep t , the simulator tracks the state s t = [ s 1 t , . . . , s n t ] of n cars including the ego-driver, where s i t = [ p i t , v i t , a i t , l i t ] contains position p = ( p x , p y ) , velocity v , acceleration a , and lane details l for vehicle i ∈ { 1 , . . . , n } . The observation o t = Z ( s t , d, ϵ ) presented to m is set by masking the components of s t according to a boolean vector d ∈ { 0 , 1 } 4 , and adding sensor noise ϵ ∈ R 4 . Each entry of d chooses whether a sensor source ( i . e ., position, velocity, acceleration, lane) is included in the observation. A prompt generator G takes the input observation and any other prompt and perturbation settings b to assemble a prompt x t = G ( o t , b ) . In our case study, b is used to (1) choose the system prompt between a preferred conservative (cons.) driving style and an aggressive (agg.) one, (2) randomize the ordering of phrases from o t , and (3) choose whether few-shot examples are fed to m . Given an assembled prompt x t we treat m as a black-box and instruct the model to directly output a discrete action a t ∈ A = { Merge Left/Right , Idle , Accelerate , Decelerate } . Example prompts are in Appendix B. Once an action has been chosen, the simulator steps to s t +1 with a transition function P unknown to m , and the agent is rewarded for driving at high speeds without collision. An episode ends after T steps, or once the ego-agent crashes.

## 4 AST Robustness Characterization

We now formulate the characterization of LLM robustness to perturbations as an AST task.

## 4.1 Adaptive Stress Testing Formulation

AST is formulated as a Markov decision process and consists of a simulator S , a reward function R ζ , and an adversarial agent ζ . As shown in Figure 3 (Left), the simulator S contains both the model-under-test (MUT) and the environment scenario to test the MUT in. The MUT is any blackbox LLM m , and the environment is a specific situation in the world to generate perturbed prompts for. We freeze the environment e at a timestep t , and analyze the responses of the LLM to perturbed prompts ˜ x t . The state of S at iteration k at this frozen time t is denoted by s t k = [ d k , ϵ k , b k ] , where d k , ϵ k , and b k are defined as in Section 3.1. At iteration k , the adversary takes a discrete action a k to update the simulator's perturbation state s t k +1 . Potential adversarial actions include masking out sensor sources with d k +1 , applying noise with ϵ k +1 , or changing prompt phrasing with b k +1 . The state s t k holds all of the information needed to track (1) which details from the original environment state s t will be provided to m through o t , and (2) how the original environment prompt x t will be perturbed. When given a perturbation action a k , we configure the next prompt settings s t k +1 , and generate N prompts { ˜ x t 1 , . . . , ˜ x t N } following the rules of s t k +1 using G . We then pass each of the prompts through m to sample N decisions a ′ t = { a ′ t 1 , . . . , a ′ t N } that lie in the action space of the environment. Finally, the adversary receives a reward r k , where R ζ is a reward function crafted to teach the adversary to find perturbation actions that increase undesired behaviors from the LLM, as discussed in Section 4.2.

## 4.2 Searching for Impactful Perturbations

The AST state space ( i . e ., number of perturbation states) grows exponentially with the size of the action space ( i . e ., number of perturbation actions). In the case of the highway environment, we allow the adversarial agent to take nine different actions: (12) set cons. or agg. system prompt; (3-6) remove an observed sensor source; (7) remove few-shot examples; (8) add noise; and (9) randomize order of prompt details. Assume that the adversarial agent selects a single system prompt configuration and may take each action at most once. If at least one sensor type must be observable, then there are 240 possible unique prompt settings. We represent different permutations of adversarial action trajectories as a tree, where a path of actions leads to a perturbation state node. 3 Even if we cache the outputs of m from previously visited perturbation states, producing just 20 trees with N = 5 samples per state requires 24 K LLM generations. This evaluation can cost researchers upwards of $100 .

Like prior AST works (Lee et al., 2015; Corso et al., 2019; Du and Driggs-Campbell, 2021), we use MCTS to intelligently search for perturbation action trajectories that maximize undesired LLM behaviors, without exploring the entire prompt perturbation space. During the search, at every previously unvisited perturbation state s t k +1 , we cache the generations { a ′ t 1 , . . . , a ′ t N } from m . ## 5 Characterization Experiments

We first characterize LLM robustness with MCTS and then use the trained trees in three applications.

Collecting a Characterization Dataset To generate a set of trees for different scenarios, we first collect rollout episodes using the MUT. Specifically, we collect ten trajectories in the highway environment using Llama, Dolphin, and Qwen (skipping DeepSeek due to slow generation speeds) with the aggressive system prompt and no perturbations.

We select the top 20 timesteps whose descriptions' BGE-M3 (Chen et al., 2024) embeddings have the lowest cosine-similarity to those of all others, although other methods could be employed to select a broad distribution of scenarios, like using state criticality (Du and Driggs-Campbell, 2021). Like many AST works, we use MCTS with double progressive widening (Lee et al., 2015) as our adversarial AST agent, although our method is agnostic to the choice of reinforcement learning algorithm. Similar to Lee et al. (2015), our state transition model is deterministic because we cache generations from the LLM when visiting new perturbation states, and only the first step of double progressive widening is performed on the action space. The adversarial agent samples five responses from the MUT at every new perturbation state, and we generate trees of varied complexities with the three choices for U introduced in Section 4.2. The Whole tree searches the entire perturbation space with 1000 iterations of MCTS down to the maximum search depth (seven actions). Representative of a resource-constrained environment, two other trees search only ∼ 25% ( 60 states) of the perturbation space with (1) 100 iterations down to a depth of five actions ( Shallow ), and (2) 64 iterations down to the maximum depth ( Deep ). Trees using L are only trained on scenarios where m can act without crashing, allowing for non-zero adversarial returns.

## Limitations

In this work, we present a general framework for stress testing language models under noisy observations and perturbed phrasings with systematically generated prompts. Below, we discuss several limitations and future directions.

First, as discussed in Section 4.1, adaptive stress testing requires a simulator that contains a MUT to evaluate in an environment. As discussed in Section 5, offline characterizations can be performed to gauge whether a MUT is right for a task. However, we also find that, if an LLM consistently hallucinates regardless of scenario, phrasing, and observation, our method will offer only limited insight into failure mode trends during applications. For example, we see that the effectiveness of prompt generations based on characterizations for uncertainty influence in Figure 9 is limited for Llama and Dolphin compared to Qwen. In the future, we aim to test other capable closed-source models in other deployments and in the real-world.

Second, developing the AST simulator requires defining the prompt perturbation space and developing a rule-based prompt generator grounded in

the observation space of the environment. We propose to split our prompt perturbation space into two dimensions spanning observed (noisy) sensor details and prompt phrasing. The former is defined by the available sensors on the system to be controlled, while the latter is formed by a researcher's imagination of how the observation could be phrased. Manually defining an appropriate set of perturbation states is nontrivial, and may vary across deployment environments. Furthermore, the rule-based prompt generator becomes more complex as more perturbations are considered. Future work could be geared towards automating the design of the prompt phrasing perturbation space and generation of a rule-based prompt generator, as long as the generator is grounded in the observation space of the system.

Third, we show examples of how offline analyses can be used for applications in unseen situations to generate prompts that influence model predictions and identify scenarios where the model is likely to act poorly. In our study, we create a relatively small dataset of prompt perturbation trees over 20 timesteps. As we were constrained by compute resources when generating Whole, Deep, and Shallow trees for multiple LLMs across multiple environments, we did not train more trees. If the MUT encounters a scenario that is very different to the ones in the dataset, the results of sampled 'similar' trees and perturbation templates may not provide informative details as to how the model is expected to act in this case. As such, a diverse distribution of dataset scenarios is required to generalize to a number of unseen scenarios. For example, in Figure 10, consistently generating undesirable prompts that cause the MUT to crash into another vehicle using L is challenging, since the test-time scenarios may not align closely with the training distribution. Characterizing with a larger dataset will assist with this issue.

Finally, in this work, we characterized LLMs' robustness in planning and control tasks. Recently, vision-language-action models (VLAs) have been developed to perform autonomous driving, robot navigation, manipulation, and other functions. In the future, we aim to stress test these models in high-fidelity simulators to gain an understanding of failure modes. Furthermore, our work currently ignores stress testing models by controlling other adversarial agents over multiple steps in an environment. Combining both observation and scenario stress testing into one framework will provide a comprehensive toolkit for understanding the limitations of models prior to deployment. In fact, we aim to merge this toolkit into a greater data flywheel to continuously generate more data and improve models through stress testing.

## Ethical Considerations

We note that, as in other adversarial generation and red-teaming works, malicious parties could use our method to design prompt perturbation spaces that elicit harmful behaviors. This possibility is further amplified by the black-box sampling approach of our method, meaning that our framework can be applied to closed-source models that malicious actors may have API access to. As such, further guardrails should be designed to limit undesired behaviors from models at runtime. We argue, however, that our method enables researchers to proactively understand potential failure cases during model development and mitigate them with further finetuning. In the case of developers who are narrowing down a set of potential (closed-source) models to utilize for their system, our framework enables them to choose a model that meets their safety specifications. This proactive stress testing is unavoidably needed in order to advance the understanding of model limitations and deploy predictable foundation model planners.

## A The Importance of Studying Observation Perturbations

In the real world, sensors are unreliable. Even with filtering, poor lighting or weather can cause false detections of other agents, controller area network (CAN) messages in vehicles could be dropped, etc . Also, the available sensors can differ from platform to platform ( e . g ., a vehicle missing a front camera, a robot without a lidar). When the MUT is expected to adapt to different platforms with varied sensors, it is critical to understand which settings break the model. Both of these cases are especially critical to consider for black-box LLMs whose training data is hidden. We choose to perturb the availability of pose, speed, etc . of vehicles/robots to simulate when detection algorithms or precise localization fail. In the lunar lander environment described in Appendix G, we perturb similar observations to emulate a developer narrowing down a set of sensors necessary for the task to be completed successfully under constraints, or to simulate if any sensors fail upon reentry.

If an LLM is found to fail under certain perturbations (even conditions that humans naturally expect models to perform poorly under), we should take action to improve test-time performance. For example, if our characterization finds that our MUT acts randomly when missing velocity data, we can choose to either rely on a safer controller ( e . g ., human driver, teleoperator), or finetune the (opensource) model to confidently predict more conservative actions that align with human preferences. Furthermore, real-time applications in Section 5 and Appendix E.3 show that our characterization dataset can be used to find worst-case scenarios that lead to undesired MUT behaviors. If left unchecked, when the model encounters such perturbations during deployment, similar failures will arise with real-world consequences. Thus, these characterizations enable developers to proactively understand the expected behavior of the MUT in varied cases and take note of which scenarios need further attention for anomaly detection or finetuning.

## D Visualizing a Perturbation Tree

In Figures 15 - 17, we visualize the process of building a prompt perturbation tree, as described in Section 4. In this example, we use action diversity D ( s t k ) -introduced as Equation 2 in Section 4.2 - as our undesirability function U ( s t k ) . Recall that the first action an adversary can take is to set the preferred driving style to either conservative or aggressive. In Figure 15, we choose to set the preferred style to be conservative. The current perturbation state is defined by the sequence of adversarial actions taken thus far to reach the current node in the perturbation tree. This perturbation state is fed to the prompt generator to produce N prompts following the rules of the prompt template. Note that because the current prompt template has not yet introduced any stochasticity into the prompt via noise or randomization, all N generated prompts will be the same for the moment. We then sample one response from the LLM per prompt and compute the diversity of generations, as described in Section 4.2. The perturbation state and its corresponding sampled generations are stored into a cache for later lookup. Undesirability functions like L that are dependent on results from a (potentially stochastic) external model ( e . g ., R m ) rely on the cached generations. In these cases, our framework inherently allows for stochasticity in the environment by recomputing U with the cached generations from the model. Finally, the reward our adversary receives is the change in undesirability from the prior state, where we let U ( s 0 ) = 0 .

In Figure 16, our agent then takes another action to remove velocity details from the prompt, in addition to setting the conservative driving style preference. Thus, the generated prompts follow both constraints. We then sample N responses from the model and compute the diversity induced by the new perturbation state. The reward the adversary receives for taking this action is the change in diversity from the prior state. Because the samples of the prior state are cached, we do not have to resample responses from the LLM to estimate the uncertainty of that state. As the undesirability of the current state can be smaller or greater than the prior state's, the reward the adversary receives can be negative or positive. Using the undesirability estimates described in Section 4.2 bounds r k ∈ [ -1 , 1] .

Figure 17 demonstrates an example of how the perturbation tree may look after several adversarial steps. Notice that the adversary can choose to take actions that branch off from earlier nodes in the tree. The N generated prompts for the current perturbation state (setting conservative prompt, removing position &amp; acceleration, and adding noise) will all be different because the prompt template has introduced stochasticity with noise. Finally, we say that a set of trees generated for different scenarios is a characterization dataset for the MUT, as it characterizes how the model may act in similar scenarios at runtime.

## D.1 Perturbation Space Complexity

Recall from Section 4.2 that the perturbation state space grows exponentially with the action space. Given the nine different actions we allow the adversary to take in the driving environment, there are 2 4 ( 2 4 -1 ) = 240 possible unique prompt settings. Consequently, there are 2 · ∑ 7 i =1 7! i ! = 17320 possible permutations of adversarial action trajectories. Here, we are assuming that the adversarial agent selects a single system prompt configuration, may take each action at most once, and at least one sensor type must be observable. Note that multiple action trajectories lead to the same underlying prompt perturbation state ( e . g ., set cons. prompt → remove position → remove velocity vs. set cons. prompt → remove velocity → remove position). We estimate that just 20 trees requires 9M input and 4.5M output tokens using Anthropic's Sonnet 4, costing researchers $95 as of September 2025 (Anthropic, 2025).

## D.2 Estimating LLM Uncertainty

As discussed in Section 4.2, we use normalized Shannon entropy and action diversity as proxies for LLM uncertainty estimation. Both H ( s t k ) and D ( s t k ) are bounded on [0 , 1] , where a diverse prediction set is assigned a higher value. Table 3 lists examples of sampled action prediction sets with their corresponding normalized action diversity and Shannon entropy values according to Equations 1 and 2 from Section 4.2. Action diversity gives a significantly higher diversity estimate once the model predicts two unique actions about equally often ( e . g ., { 1 , 1 , 1 , 2 , 2 } , { 2 , 3 , 3 , 4 , 4 } ). In contrast, normalized Shannon entropy provides a steadier increase in uncertainty estimate across varying prediction samples.

## E AST Driving Experiment Details

Here, we detail the implementation of our method and experiments in the driving environment and provide additional results.

## E.1 Implementation

We use the publicly available AST toolbox (v2020.09.01.7) (SISL, 2020) to deploy MCTS with double progressive widening (Lee et al., 2015) as our adversarial AST agent. Similar to Lee et al.

(2015), our state transition model is deterministic because we cache generations from the LLM when visiting new perturbation states, and only the first step of double progressive widening is performed on the action space. Each of the four LLMs are hosted on an Ollama (v0.6.8) (Ollama, 2025) server. To enable efficient characterization, we set Ollama to host as many copies of the MUT that can fit on our hardware at one time, such that queries can be fulfilled in parallel. Our compute platform runs the RHEL 8.8 operating system and is comprised of one AMD 7763 CPU with 64 cores, one NVIDIA A40 GPU with 48 GB memory, and 32 GB RAM. Finally, we designed a custom prompt generator that is inspired by the one from Wen et al. (2024a), which can take an observation from the highway environment (Leurent, 2018) and a set of perturbation settings d k , ϵ k , and b k (described in Section 3.1) to generate perturbed prompts. This generator is incorporated into our AST simulator as discussed in Section 4.1. Our simulator is designed to use the open-source AnythingLLM (MintplexLabs, 2025) library as a bridge between the MUT and the AST algorithm. As such, other models ( e . g ., closed-source LLMs) can be easily evaluated by modifying minimal code because of its unified API for querying different models.

## F Robot Crowd Navigation Experiment Details

Here, we detail the implementation of the robot crowd navigation environment and discuss the perturbation space and undesirability function used.

## F.1 Simulator Setup

We adapt the robot crowd navigation simulator presented by Liu et al. (2023) for our purposes. Figure 24 visualizes one timestep in the simulator. Each agent in the simulator is holonomic and observes the nearby agents within some set sensor radius. They are each initialized with a random goal position within the environment arena, and at every timestep, they take a 2D velocity action to attempt to reach the goal while avoiding colliding with other agents. Each human agent is controlled by an Optimal Reciprocal Collision Avoidance (ORCA) (van den Berg et al., 2011) policy. We initialize the simulator with 20 humans and one robot (each represented as a circle of radius 0 . 3 ) positioned around a circle of radius 8 . 5 in a square arena. Each agent is assigned a goal at the opposite end of the circle with some perturbations. Additionally, each agent can observe a 360 ◦ field around itself up to a sensor range of 5 . We refer readers to the work by Liu et al. (2023) for other details on the simulator implementation.

## G.1 Simulator Setup

We adapt the lunar lander simulator presented by Towers et al. (2024) for our purposes. Figure 25 visualizes one timestep in the simulator. At every new episode, the simulator is initialized such that the lander is placed above the randomly generated surface with some random position, heading, and velocity. We introduce a stochastic state transition function by applying a random wind force before taking a given action. Thus, repeated steps from the same state with the same action will stochastically lead to different next states and rewards. This environment has a discrete action space where the agent can choose to fire one of the main, left, or right thrusters, or turn off all engines. Only one thruster is allowed to be on at any time. Additionally, the gravity on the moon is -10 m/s 2 and the goal is always positioned at (0 , 0) .
