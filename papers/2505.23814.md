## Position: Watermarking Without Standards Is Not AI Governance

Alexander Nemecek 1 Yuzhou Jiang 2 Erman Ayday 1

## Abstract

Watermarking has emerged as a leading technical proposal for attributing generative AI content and is increasingly cited in global governance frameworks. This position paper argues that current implementations risk serving as symbolic compliance rather than delivering effective oversight. We identify a growing gap between regulatory expectations and the technical limitations of existing watermarking schemes. Through analysis of policy proposals and industry practices, we show how incentive structures disincentivize robust, auditable deployments. To realign watermarking with governance goals, we propose a three-layer framework encompassing technical standards, audit infrastructure, and enforcement mechanisms. Without enforceable requirements and independent verification, watermarking will remain inadequate for accountability and ultimately undermine broader efforts in AI safety and regulation.

## 1. Introduction

'A law without teeth is just a suggestion. '

This adage captures a growing concern in AI governance, where policies are advancing more quickly than the technical tools available to enforce them. A prominent example of this mismatch is watermarking , a family of techniques designed to embed identifiable signatures into AI-generated content. Watermarking is widely referenced in AI governance frameworks, yet current implementations often lack the robustness, transparency, and verifiability required to fulfill this role effectively.

Recent advances in generative AI have significantly increased the scale and realism of synthetic content, including text, images, and audio (Spennemann, 2025; Fisher et al., 2024). As such content is deployed in sensitive areas like education, healthcare, and finance, policymakers have identified attribution and provenance as urgent challenges. Misattributed content contributes to the spread of misinformation and to technical failures such as feedback loops in model training, including contamination and eventual model collapse (Shumailov et al., 2024).

In response, watermarking has gained traction in both technical research (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Fernandez et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) and policymaking. The United States Executive Order 14110, for example, mandates 'stateof-the-art' provenance tools and cites watermarking explicitly (Exe, 2023). The European Union's AI Act requires machine-readable content markings (EUA, 2024), with similar provisions being proposed in jurisdictions worldwide (Zhao et al., 2024a). Although watermarking is just one of several provenance strategies, it occupies an important role in current governance discourse.

This reliance rests on a flawed foundation. While watermarking techniques vary across modalities, most remain brittle, difficult to audit, and proprietary. Policymakers often assume these methods can be standardized and verified. In practice, industry deployments obscure technical details while asserting compliance, turning watermarking into a box-checking exercise rather than a meaningful tool. Lacking common standards, evaluation infrastructure, or defined threat models, current implementations are unlikely to fulfill their intended governance role.

To address this growing disconnect, we advance two central positions:

- Watermarking schemes must be designed with verifiability and auditability as primary technical requirements, rather than implemented as proprietary black boxes.
- Policymakers must establish technical standards and independent testing to ensure that watermarking fulfills governance goals in practice.

To support these positions, we make three contributions. First, we analyze emerging watermarking mandates and demonstrate they assume levels of technical feasibility that current systems do not meet. Second, we examine the incentive structures driving industry watermarking deployments, showing how market dynamics often disincentivize robust, auditable implementations. Third, we propose a three-layer framework, spanning technical design, audit infrastructure, and enforcement which realigns the design of watermarking systems with their intended governance functions.

While prior work has analyzed watermarking's technical constraints, our contribution reframes these issues through a governance lens and provides a structured, actionable framework for aligning technical design with regulatory intent. Without intervention at the intersection of policy and design, watermarking is unlikely to deliver meaningful accountability. Instead, it risks becoming a symbolic substitute for the more demanding components of effective AI regulation.

## 2. Governance Aspirations vs. Technical Realities

As watermarking becomes a feature of AI governance proposals, many mandates rely on technical assumptions that current systems cannot fulfill. We identify three such assumptions and contrast each policy aspiration with technical realities, highlighting key gaps that must be addressed for watermarking to serve as an effective governance tool.

## 2.1. Assumption I: Watermarking Robustness Can Be Required Without Being Defined

Many governance proposals call for watermarking that is robust against tampering, yet leave undefined what level of robustness satisfies this requirement. Whether applied to text, images, or audio, watermarking is expected to remain detectable throughout the content lifecycle, but no threshold specifies how much degradation is acceptable.

'The disclosure is permanent or extraordinarily difficult to remove, to the extent it is technically feasible. ' -California Senate Bill No. 942 (California State Legislature, 2024)

'Such techniques and methods should be sufficiently reliable, interoperable, effective and robust as far as this is technically feasible, taking into account available techniques or a combination of such techniques, such as watermarks,. . . as may be appropriate.' - European Union AI Act (EUA, 2024)

These documents reflect the shared policy expectation of robustness but delegate its meaning to phrases such as 'technically feasible,' providing no operational guidance. Without quantitative thresholds or shared metrics for evaluating robustness under adversarial conditions, meaningful compliance remains unverifiable and enforcement difficult.

In practice, watermarking schemes often fall short of this robustness ideal. Google's SynthID (Google DeepMind, 2023), for example, embeds imperceptible watermarks in AI-generated text. While resilient to certain distortions, its detection can be evaded by simple edits such as character perturbations or short-form text paraphrasing (Dathathri et al., 2024; Nemecek et al., 2024). Across all modalities, there is currently no standardized evaluation protocol for watermark robustness, and few systems offer guarantees under adversarial or worst-case conditions.

The absence of shared benchmarks or test suites for robustness creates two problems: first, it makes meaningful regulatory compliance unverifiable; second, it enables firms to implement weak watermarking schemes while claiming alignment with policy language.

To concretely illustrate this gap in industry, we conducted minimal robustness evaluations across two modalities using publicly deployed watermarking systems: Google's SynthID-Text (Dathathri et al., 2024) for text and Meta's AudioSeal (Roman et al., 2024) for audio. SynthIDText is deployed in Gemini, but direct access to detection scores is unavailable; we therefore evaluate on Gemma-2-9b-it (Gemma Team, 2024), Google's opensource model family. Similarly, AudioSeal has been deployed in Meta's public-facing AI products as a post-hoc watermarking system applied to generated audio (Fernandez, 2025), but we evaluate on AudioGen (Kreuk et al., 2022) as an accessible proxy.

For each modality, we generated five outputs and evaluated three variants. Text outputs were tested in original, paraphrased, and summarized forms, with transformations applied using GPT-4o (Hurst et al., 2024). Audio outputs were tested in original form, with modified playback speed, and with additive noise. These transformations reflect common user modifications across both modalities. Detection thresholds were obtained from published documentation of each system (Dathathri et al., 2024; Roman et al., 2024). As shown in Table 1, even benign edits frequently degraded detection scores below threshold across both modalities: while 9 of 10 original outputs were detected, only 1 of 20 transformed outputs remained detectable. Full evaluation details, prompts, and outputs are provided in Appendix B.

These statements imply that watermarking should support reliable, externally verifiable detection. Most watermarking systems today do not support this expectation due either to limited transparency or configuration dependence.

For instance, OpenAI previously released a classifier for detecting AI-generated text, but withdrew it due to unreliability (OpenAI, 2023). Google's SynthID offers a more complete approach, with watermarking and detection tools for images and text. SynthID-Text (Dathathri et al., 2024) supports watermarking and detection in public tools, but detection still requires access to the configuration used during generation. Additionally, users need to apply to receive access to the main configurations used in deployment. Unless watermarking keys or models are shared or standardized, third-party detection remains limited to contexts explicitly designed for interoperability.

While the technical foundations for third-party detection are emerging, the practical reality remains constrained. Most watermarking approaches do not currently support universal, auditable detection, and governance frameworks often overlook the infrastructure and standardization needed to make detection viable at scale.

## 2.3. Assumption III: Industry Will Voluntarily Align with Governance Goals

A final assumption in governance frameworks, particularly in the United States and European Union, is that generative AI providers will voluntarily adopt watermarking practices aligned with policy goals.

'. . . establish a strong international framework for managing the risks and harnessing the benefits of AI, including by encouraging international allies and partners to support voluntary commitments similar to those that United States companies have made in pursuit of these objectives. . . to develop common regulatory and other accountability principles for foreign nations, including to manage the risk that AI systems pose. ' -United States Executive Order 14110 (Exe, 2023)

'All stakeholders, including industry, academia, civil society and standardisation organisations, are encouraged to take into account, as appropriate, the ethical principles for the development of voluntary best practices and standards. ' -European Union AI Act (EUA, 2024)

This reflects a broader strategy of governance-by-consensus, based on reputational pressure and shared norms. The United States Biden-Harris Administration secured voluntary commitments from seven leading AI companies explicitly referencing watermarking to lead safe and transparent AI (White House, 2023a). An additional group of eight companies later joined these commitments (White House, 2023b).

However, implementation remains fragmented, with many deployments being firm-specific and lacking interoperability. Few are publicly auditable or developed through shared infrastructure. In the absence of enforceable standards or independent oversight, these commitments risk enabling symbolic compliance, signaling safety while delivering little practical governance capability.

Voluntary alignment also depends on political continuity. In 2025, the United States Trump-Vance Administration rescinded Executive Order 14110, directing agencies to 'suspend, revise, or rescind' related initiatives (White House, 2025). The administration's stated focus on technological competitiveness leaves the governance status of watermark- ing ambiguous, casting uncertainty over the future of watermarking as a governance tool. By contrast, China's Deep Synthesis Provisions (CAC, 2022) offer a more directive model, mandating compliance and audits, but even in such regimes, enforcement is uneven and robustness is not guaranteed.

While voluntary commitments may promote cooperation, they offer a fragile foundation for governance. When watermarking imposes costs or strategic risks, firms are unlikely to sustain alignment in the absence of legal or institutional compulsion.

## 3. Why the Gap Persists: Industry Incentives

Despite growing regulatory attention, industry watermarking implementations remain fragmented. This is not simply a technical lag but reflects a deeper misalignment between governance objectives and industry incentives. Without enforceable standards, firms have strong incentives to pursue symbolic or minimal compliance (Aaronson, 2024).

First, watermarking offers reputational value. Companies can signal alignment with governance goals by announcing watermarking initiatives, often without disclosing technical details or enabling verification. This satisfies public demands at low cost, while sidestepping the risks of committing to truly robust solutions.

Second, robust watermarking entails economic and strategic risks. If detection tools are open source, they can be exploited by modifying outputs to evade detection or crafting content that falsely appears watermarked. Conversely, restricted-access to detection capabilities would require companies to develop secure infrastructure to manage watermarking keys, detection thresholds, and access control via cryptographic or trust-based frameworks. These trade-offs make firms hesitant to invest in public, auditable watermarking systems that support third-party verification.

Third, companies face disincentives to move unilaterally. Without coordination, early adopters risk losing users if watermarking is perceived as restrictive, such as for moderation or IP enforcement, especially if competitors offer unmarked alternatives. In a rapidly evolving market, the incentive to retain users outweighs alignment with long-term governance objectives.

Finally, regulatory uncertainty undermines long-term planning, further amplifying industry hesitation. As seen with the revocation of Executive Order 14110 (White House, 2025), shifting political priorities can quickly alter the expected policy landscape. In such an environment, companies are hesitant to invest in watermarking infrastructure that may soon be obsolete.

Together, these forces collectively encourage minimal, firm- specific, and performative watermarking environments, an outcome misaligned with the goals of effective AI oversight.

## 4. A Three-Layer Approach to Enforceable Watermarking

To bridge the gap between policy ambition and technical feasibility, we propose a three-layer framework: (I) technical requirements, (II) audit infrastructure, and (III) policy enforcement. Each layer targets a key weakness in current watermarking practices and together, they establish the groundwork for end-to-end accountability. Figure 1 summarizes the framework.

## 4.1. Technical Requirements

Effective governance depends on watermarking systems that are technically sound and externally testable. At a minimum, watermarking methods should demonstrate robustness to both benign transformations and adversarial modifications designed to evade detection. Systems must report standardized performance metrics, including detection accuracy, false positive rates, and robustness under defined perturbation sets. Crucially, these guarantees should not require full transparency of proprietary model internals but must expose interfaces and artifacts, such as detectors, keys, or hashes, that enable reproducible evaluation by third parties. This layer establishes the minimum technical properties needed to claim that watermarking is doing more than signaling intent.

Watermarking spans multiple generative modalities, including text, image, and audio (Zhao et al., 2024a). Each modality requires distinct robustness evaluations to assess watermark persistence under realistic conditions. Robustness refers to a watermark's ability to withstand both benign transformations (e.g., compression, summarization) and adversarial modifications (e.g., targeted perturbations designed to evade detection). Layer 1 calls for unified, reproducible technical standards to evaluate watermark durability against such transformations.

Each modality exhibits specific transformations that risk watermark integrity:

- Text: paraphrasing, spelling alterations, lexical substitutions, summarization, and machine translation (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023).
- Image: lossy compression (e.g., JPEG), geometric manipulations (cropping, rotation, scaling), color shifts, and style transfer (Zhao et al., 2024b).
- Audio: background noise injection, pitch shifts, reencoding, and time-stretching (Chen et al., 2023).

A watermarking scheme must be evaluated against these transformations not only for robustness, but also for recoverability without false attribution.

The research community has made meaningful progress toward systematizing watermark evaluation. Benchmarking efforts such as MarkMyWords (Piet et al., 2025) (text), WAVES (An et al., 2024) (images), and AudioMarkBench (Liu et al., 2024) (audio) provide standardized robustness evaluations across modalities, while a growing body of work examines attack vectors including removal, forgery, and spoofing (Liang et al., 2025; Jovanovi´ c et al., 2024). While these contributions offer valuable technical foundations, they remain research artifacts rather than governance infrastructure. They are adopted in academic settings but not integrated into compliance pathways or industry processes. Bridging this gap requires not new benchmarks per se, but the institutionalization of benchmarking within enforceable governance frameworks.

Wesuggest the creation of a shared benchmarking library for generative watermarking evaluation, accessible to industry, regulators, and auditors. Such a library would consolidate existing transformation pipelines standardized across modalities into centralized infrastructure with automated reporting of scores. Performance metrics should draw from those established in prior work, at minimum, detection accuracy under perturbation and false positive/negative rates. Watermarking deployments cannot tolerate mislabeling human content as AI-generated, hence metrics must emphasize performance at low false positive rates (FPR), such as true positive rate (TPR) at 1% FPR or 0.1% FPR, or equivalently, partial area under the ROC curve (AUC) truncated at these thresholds. Where applicable, modality-specific metrics should also be reported.

This infrastructure would be centrally maintained and periodically updated to reflect new attack strategies and model capabilities. In practice, this would enable both independent researchers and regulatory agencies to evaluate watermarking claims reproducibly across diverse systems. Systems must also document tradeoffs, such as reductions in model output quality, inference latency increases, or decreased robustness to noise, as well as the specific watermark strength and threshold configurations intended for deployment. This transparency allows users and auditors to understand the operational impact of embedding techniques and verify that certified parameters match production settings. To stay ahead of emerging threats, the library should also support contributions from red-teaming initiatives and adversarial research challenges, ensuring that benchmark suites evolve alongside attack capabilities (Ahmad et al., 2025; Anthropic, 2025).

While Executive Order 14110 (Exe, 2023) identified the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) as a natural candidate for benchmarking watermarking technologies, the pace of AI system deployment exceeds NIST's current update cycles. We recommend that NIST serve as the registry and certifier, while operational development of the library should be delegated to a more agile, communitydriven consortium. This hybrid structure would combine institutional legitimacy with rapid iteration and responsiveness to evolving technical landscapes.

To institutionalize this layer, any system claiming compliance must submit its watermarking pipeline to this library's API for evaluation. Recognizing potential concerns over intellectual property or security, alternative compliance paths such as exposing a detection API or providing zeroknowledge proofs could be considered, provided they allow equivalent third-party evaluation. Outputs will be scored on standardized perturbation suites, with results logged in a public registry. This shifts evaluation from self-reported metrics to verifiable compliance with shared robustness expectations. Such a system ensures that claims of watermark durability are not merely assertions, but demonstrable properties grounded in reproducible tests. These reproducible evaluations also form the empirical basis for audit infrastructure in Layer 2, enabling certifiers to assess compliance without needing privileged access.

## 4.2. Audit Infrastructure

Robust watermarking requires more than technical performance as it must be verifiable through independent and reproducible evaluation. This layer establishes the infrastructure for third-party audits, including standardized black-box testing protocols, shared testbeds, and recognized certifying bodies. Audits should reflect real-world use conditions rather than idealized lab settings and must work across different vendors without requiring access to proprietary internals. Systems should expose externally testable behaviors or cryptographic commitments that allow verification without reverse engineering. By enabling interoperable, independent testing, this layer transforms watermarking from internal assurance into a publicly accountable mechanism.

To verify that deployed watermarking schemes meet the minimal technical requirements outlined in Layer 1, robust audit systems must be established. However, watermarking must remain resilient to perturbations while concealing key detection parameters. Full public access to a detection system would enable adversaries to remove or forge watermarks, undermining the scheme's integrity. Consequently, audit systems must operate in a black-box setting where evaluators do not access model internals, but instead submit test content and observe binary or probabilistic detection outcomes. This approach introduces challenges since blackbox detectors are vulnerable to repeated querying attacks, where adversaries iteratively probe a system to learn its boundaries (Sadasivan et al., 2023). To mitigate this, detection interfaces must implement access controls, either via restrained queries or containerized deployments (Tram` er et al., 2016). These include vendor-specific configurations such as closed-source APIs or on-premise audit tools, depending on the operational context or vendor policy.

Watermarking deployments should also support cryptographic commitments that allow external verification without revealing sensitive internals. This can involve traditional hash-based attestations (e.g., SHA-256 commitments to watermark parameters) (Pub, 2012) or emerging cryptographic techniques such as zero-knowledge proofs (Sasson et al., 2014). For example, a system might prove to an auditor that it conforms to a certified detection threshold, without disclosing the underlying keys or watermark design.

Acentral question concerns auditor identity and governance. Potential certifiers include NIST-accredited laboratories, third-party security firms, or consortia established for compliance auditing (AI, 2023). Certification levels may include baseline compliance for systems meeting minimum robustness, interoperability certification for systems supporting cross-vendor detection, or adversarial resilience certification for schemes robust under threat-model testing. Audit cadence is equally critical. Evaluations should occur: (i) pre-deployment, to certify governance readiness, (ii) periodically post-deployment, to catch degradation or drift, and (iii) post-incident, in response to adversarial exploits or system failures. As adversaries evolve, the audit layer must ensure that claims of durability and detectability remain valid over time. Auditors must be able to reproduce robustness metrics defined in Layer 1 using only externally exposed interfaces. This includes performance on benchmark perturbation sets, detection behavior under adversarial conditions, and public system documentation. Certified systems should be logged in a public registry indicating audit status and any revocations or compliance failures. While audit processes introduce operational overhead, they establish trust by linking technical claims to external validation. Layer 2 builds directly on the technical guarantees of Layer 1, ensuring they are externally verified, reproducible, and trustworthy in deployment contexts. ## 4.3. Policy and Enforcement

The final layer ensures that technical and audit standards translate into real-world accountability. Without legal mandates and institutional enforcement, even robust and verifiable watermarking systems may go unused or implemented inconsistently. These mechanisms should be tied to existing governance processes, such as model registration or domain-specific safety requirements. Public disclosures, for instance, in model cards, help standardize expectations and support auditability. Certification should be required for deployment in high-risk contexts, and penalties should apply for unverifiable or misleading claims. This layer connects the technical and audit layers to institutional incentives, closing the loop from design to deployment.

To ensure that technical requirements and audit mechanisms translate into industry commitment, enforceable legal and regulatory consequences must be in place. Without binding consequences, the outputs of Layers 1 and 2 risk being symbolic, allowing firms to claim compliance without delivering accountability. Layer 3 operationalizes these layers by anchoring them in policy frameworks that mandate compliance and impose consequences for deviation.

Certification based on audit results should be required for deployment in high-governance contexts (e.g., elections, education, public interfaces). Systems that fail certification would face deployment restrictions or public disclosure of non-compliance. In tandem, mandated disclosures in system documentation and model cards must include audit status, robustness guarantees, and known failure modes (Mitchell et al., 2019). These disclosures provide transparency while enabling downstream accountability. Non-compliant systems failing to meet audit standards or refusing audit participation should face graduated enforcement actions. These may include fines, removal of deployment licenses for regulated sectors, or public listing in non-compliance registries. For firms, such penalties also carry reputational risk, further incentivizing alignment with certification pathways.

Enforcement authority must be clearly defined. Governance bodies such as NIST (United States) (Exe, 2023), designated regulators under the European Union AI Act (EUA, 2024), or China's Cyberspace Administration (CAC, 2022) can serve as enforcement agents within their jurisdictions. However, because AI systems operate across borders, enforcement must also address international fragmentation. A model deployed in one jurisdiction, producing outputs accessible in another, would make alignment across regimes critical for consistent governance.

Finally, policy enforcement must evolve alongside the underlying technologies. Just as audit protocols adapt to new adversarial techniques, enforcement mechanisms must include provisions for periodic policy review and revision. Feedback loops from auditors, researchers, and affected stakeholders can help keep regulatory frameworks responsive and legitimate. By linking technical performance and audit compliance to real-world consequences, Layer 3 closes the loop from design to deployment. It ensures that watermarking systems are not merely well-engineered, but meaningfully accountable in practice.

## 5. Alternative Views

While we argue for enforceable watermarking as a critical step toward meaningful AI governance to transform it from a symbolic gesture into a mandatory mechanism, it is im- portant to acknowledge opposing perspectives that highlight real implementation challenges.

Our proposed three-layer framework, though necessary for governance, poses substantial practical hurdles. The technical complexity alone requires the development of robust watermarking schemes across modalities, supported by standardized benchmarks and test protocols. Beyond technical design, the framework calls for the creation of new institutions to oversee audit infrastructure and certification processes. These demands would foreseeably slow down the current rapid pace of generative AI innovation, particularly for smaller firms and open-source communities.

In addition to technical challenges, the fragmented landscape of both AI development and global regulation limits the interoperability and enforceability of watermarking systems. Proprietary models often adopt incompatible watermarking implementations, while open-source forks can bypass governance requirements entirely. On the regulatory side, jurisdictions such as the United States, European Union, and People's Republic of China are developing divergent standards, ranging from voluntary commitments to strict mandates, resulting in conflicting requirements. A model compliant in one region may not meet the standards of another, undermining global accountability efforts.

Amid this fragmentation, some would argue that voluntary compliance and soft norms offer a more flexible and innovation-friendly alternative to rigid enforcement. As noted in Section 2.3, shifts in United States policy have demonstrated this volatility while one administration secured voluntary commitments from major AI firms, a subsequent administration rescinded those efforts. For some, this approach appears more politically viable than establishing binding standards. However, we contend that voluntary commitments alone are insufficient. In the absence of enforceable requirements and independent audits, companies are likely to implement minimal or symbolic watermarking measures that fall short of supporting meaningful oversight.

## 6. Conclusion

Watermarking is rapidly becoming a pillar of AI governance, but without enforceable standards and verifiable implementation, it risks becoming a symbol of oversight rather than a mechanism of accountability. We argue that robustness, verifiability, and auditability must be built into watermarking from the ground up and not added as compliance afterthoughts. Realizing this vision requires a structural shift for clear technical baselines, independent audit infrastructure, and regulatory enforcement that binds them together. Achieving durable accountability will depend on sustained collaboration between policymakers, industry actors, and the technical research community.

## A. Call to Action

Section 4 specifies what must change; this appendix addresses who should act, when , and in what sequence . We distinguish near-term steps from medium- to long-term objectives and highlight dependencies across stakeholders.

## A.1. Sequencing and Dependencies

Progress requires coordinated movement, but not all actors can move simultaneously. Standards bodies must move first, convening working groups and designating provisional benchmarks from existing academic infrastructure. As there exist current benchmarks in the literature, this foundational work is achievable within the first year. This enables policymakers to reference shared benchmarks in regulatory guidance rather than relying on undefined terms. Drawing from different benchmarking efforts, regulators can specify quantitative thresholds, for example, requiring at least 90% true positive rates at 1% false positive rates post-transformation, and industry actors can begin voluntary submission to evaluation suites. Enforcement mechanisms for high-risk domains and cross-jurisdictional mutual recognition agreements represent medium-term objectives, likely requiring two to four years of coordinated effort modeled on existing frameworks in product safety and financial regulation.

This sequencing reflects a practical constraint: enforceable mandates require shared benchmarks, and shared benchmarks require initial research and standards coordination.

## A.2. Stakeholder-Specific Priorities

Each stakeholder occupies a distinct position in the chain described in Section 4, with corresponding strategic priorities.

Standards Bodies. Candidate benchmarks already exist, such as MarkMyWords (Piet et al., 2025) for text, WA VES (An et al., 2024) for images, and AudioMarkBench (Liu et al., 2024) for audio. The near-term task is not to create new benchmarks but to consolidate these into a unified evaluation suite with standardized APIs and reporting formats. Organizations such as ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42 (the international standards committee in the area of AI), NIST, or domain-specific bodies like IEEE could serve as institutional homes. A lighter-weight alternative would be a multi-stakeholder consortium, modeled on MLCommons for ML benchmarking, that maintains the library while formal standards bodies provide certification authority.

Policymakers. For example, the EU AI Act's requirement that watermarks be 'sufficiently robust...as far as this is technically feasible' could be operationalized as follows: Watermarking systems deployed in high-risk contexts must demonstrate ≥ 90% true positive rate at ≤ 1% false positive rate on the [named benchmark suite, version X] perturbation tests, with results verified by an accredited auditor. Such specificity transforms compliance from a matter of interpretation into a testable condition. Policymakers should also define escalation pathways for systems that fail certification, ranging from mandatory disclosure to deployment restrictions in regulated sectors.

Industry. Concrete near-term actions include: (1) publishing robustness metrics against standardized perturbation suites in model cards or system documentation; (2) exposing detection endpoints, whether public APIs with rate limiting or on-premise audit tools, that allow third-party verification without revealing proprietary keys; and (3) participating in pre-competitive consortia to develop shared detection infrastructure, reducing duplication and enabling interoperability. Firms that move early can also propose benchmark extensions reflecting real-world deployment conditions, ensuring that eventual standards reflect operational realities rather than purely academic threat models.

Researchers. Two contributions fall squarely within the academic domain: (1) living benchmarks that evolve with attack capabilities, preventing the obsolescence of static test suites; and (2) governance-aware design , building watermarking schemes from inception to support auditability and interoperability rather than retrofitting these properties as compliance afterthoughts. Governance-aware design means, for instance, building watermarking schemes that output cryptographic commitments (e.g., hashes of detection parameters) at generation time, enabling later verification that a deployed system matches its certified configuration without exposing the parameters themselves.

Funding. Realizing this infrastructure requires dedicated funding. Standards maintenance, benchmark updates, and independent audits are ongoing costs that neither academia nor individual firms are well-positioned to sustain alone. Public funding agencies (e.g., NSF, European Commission) and industry consortia should jointly resource this shared infrastructure, treating it as a public good analogous to measurement standards in other regulated domains.

No single stakeholder can close the governance gap alone. Standards enable audits, audits inform enforcement, and enforcement creates incentives for robust implementation. The framework succeeds only when each actor takes plausible steps within their domain while coordinating across dependencies.

## B.1. Text: Google SynthID-Text Evaluation

We evaluate Google's SynthID-Text watermarking scheme using the open-source MarkLLM framework (Pan et al., 2024). Although SynthID-Text is deployed in Google's proprietary Gemini models, direct access to detection scores is unavailable through Gemini's chat interface or API; detection results are returned only as binary classifications without confidence values. This limitation also applies to Google's SynthID implementations for images and audio, which operate as black boxes providing only binary 'likely AI-generated' outputs. The lack of granular detection scores in these modalities precludes the quantitative robustness analysis we conduct here. In contrast, the open-sourced SynthID-Text implementation exposes detection scores, enabling systematic evaluation under transformation.

For our evaluation model, we select Gemma-2-9b-it (Gemma Team, 2024). This choice reflects three considerations: Gemma 2 is Google's relatively recent open-source model family, providing alignment with SynthID-Text's intended deployment context; the 9B parameter size balances model capability with computational accessibility; and the instructiontuned (it) variant reflects realistic deployment settings where users interact with chat-optimized models. Following the original SynthID-Text implementation (Dathathri et al., 2024) and the MarkLLM framework (Pan et al., 2024), we use a detection threshold of 0.52, classifying scores at or above this value as watermarked.

## B.2. Audio: Meta AudioSeal Evaluation

We evaluate Meta's AudioSeal (Roman et al., 2024), a post-hoc audio watermarking scheme designed for proactive detection of AI-generated speech and audio. Meta has deployed watermarking across its AI products for images, video, and audio content; however, while watermarks are reportedly applied to generated media, the specific schemes used for images and video are not publicly documented. AudioSeal is the only scheme Meta has explicitly identified as being applied to audio generation in their public-facing AI products (Fernandez, 2025).

For audio generation, we use AudioGen (Kreuk et al., 2022), an open-source text-to-audio model. This choice parallels our reasoning for selecting Gemma in the text evaluation: AudioGen was evaluated in the original AudioSeal work and represents an accessible proxy for proprietary audio generation systems where AudioSeal may be deployed. Each audio sample was generated as a 10-second clip and saved in WAV format, as specified by the AudioSeal implementation requirements. Table 4 lists the five text descriptions used as prompts for AudioGen . Following the original AudioSeal implementation (Roman et al., 2024), we use a detection threshold of 0.5, where scores at or above this value indicate watermark presence.

The selected transformations (1.25 × playback speed and 10dB noise addition) simulate realistic scenarios where users apply simple modifications to audio content. Speed adjustment is common when sharing clips on social media or adjusting for time constraints, while background noise may be introduced through re-recording, compression artifacts, or environmental interference. Importantly, these transformations are deliberately non-adversarial; they represent routine user behavior rather than sophisticated attacks designed to evade detection. As shown in Table 1, even these benign modifications substantially degrade watermark detectability, with speed modification proving particularly effective at evading detection across all samples.
